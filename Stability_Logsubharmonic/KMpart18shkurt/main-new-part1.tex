\begin{abstract}
We prove quantitative stability results for sharp hyperbolic concentration
(Faber--Krahn type) inequalities in Bergman-type settings associated with the
automorphism group of the unit ball. The main new result is the sharp planar
stability theorem for positive real-analytic log-subharmonic functions in the
weighted Bergman class $\mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D)$: if
$\Omega\subset\D$ has fixed hyperbolic measure and $(g,\Omega)$ nearly
saturates the sharp concentration inequality, then
\[
\inf_{\substack{|c|=\|g\|_{2,\alpha}\\ |a|<1}}
\frac{\|g-cg_a\|_{2,\alpha}}{\|g\|_{2,\alpha}}
\le
C_{\alpha,\tau(\Omega)}\,\delta(g;\Omega)^{1/2}.
\]
The exponent $1/2$ is optimal, already within the zero-free holomorphic
subclass.

The proof first obtains the rearrangement-and-peak estimate with exponent
$1/4$, then replaces the peak-only step by a local rigidity theory for
real-analytic log-subharmonic perturbations. The key ingredients are a
free-boundary Schur-complement computation for the bathtub optimizer, a
Green-potential absorption mechanism, a compactness theorem for normalized
Green kernels, and a nonlinear one-sided Green-gap lemma which rules out
hidden logarithmic spikes. We also prove vector-valued holomorphic Bergman
stability with the sharp exponent $1/2$, uniformly in the target dimension,
and higher-dimensional hyperbolic and Gaussian/Fock analogues obtained by the
general rearrangement method.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Rigidity and stability for concentration inequalities}

Sharp concentration inequalities in time--frequency analysis and complex function spaces typically
exhibit a strong rigidity phenomenon: equality is attained by a low-dimensional family of
extremizers obtained from a single profile by the symmetries of the ambient geometry, and
\emph{near}-equality forces the data to lie quantitatively close to that extremal manifold.
This principle has been developed in the Euclidean phase-space setting for the short-time Fourier
transform and related transforms, and in holomorphic Bergman-type spaces; see, for instance,
\cite{GomezGuerraRamosTilli2024,GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025} and the references therein.

The guiding point of this paper is that the stability exponent depends strongly
on the analytic structure of the admissible class. In holomorphic Bergman
spaces the sharp exponent is $1/2$. A purely rearrangement-and-peak argument
for log-subharmonic data gives a weaker exponent, $1/(n+2)$ in dimension $n$,
and in particular $1/4$ in the disk. The main point of the present version is
that, in the planar real-analytic log-subharmonic class, this loss is only an
artifact of the peak-only step: the true global exponent is again the sharp
rate $1/2$.

\subsection{Vector-valued holomorphic stability in the disk}

We begin with the vector-valued holomorphic case on the unit disk $\D$.
Assuming the scalar stability estimates in $\mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D)$ proved in
\cite{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025}, we show that the \emph{same} constants yield stability for
$\C^N$-valued holomorphic functions in $\mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D;\C^N)$, uniformly in $N$
(Theorem~\ref{lem:vector-stability-same-constants}). In particular, the sharp exponent $1/2$
persists in the vector-valued setting:
small deficit implies closeness to an extremizer of the form $\mathbf f_\omega(\cdot)\,v$ with rate
$\delta^{1/2}$. We also derive two useful corollaries with the same stability estimate: a modulus
stability statement for $|f|$, and a ``square-sum'' stability statement for Hilbert envelopes
$G(z)=\big(\sum_{j=1}^N|f_j(z)|^2\big)^{1/2}$.

The vector-valued proof follows the strategy of \cite{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025}:
a rearrangement reduction at the level of the scalar density $\|F\|_{\C^N}^2$, combined with a
quantitative reproducing-kernel (RKHS) argument to pass from peak control to norm distance.

\subsection{Log-subharmonic stability in hyperbolic geometry}

We then turn to stability in hyperbolic (ball) geometry for a larger class of functions than
holomorphic ones. We work on the unit ball $\mathbb B^n$ endowed with its hyperbolic structure and
with the conformal automorphism group $\mathcal M$. Following \cite{kalaj2024,kalajramos2024}, we
consider the monoid of real-analytic functions whose logarithm is $\mathcal M$-subharmonic, and the
associated weighted $\mathcal M$--Bergman spaces $\mathbf B_\alpha^p$ defined using the invariant
measure $d\tau$ and the radial weight $\Phi_n^\alpha$. In this setting one has a sharp Faber--Krahn
type inequality: among all measurable sets of fixed hyperbolic measure, hyperbolic balls maximize
the normalized concentration functional, and the extremal functions are generated by the action of
$\mathcal M$ on a single radial profile \cite{RamosTilli,kalajramos2024}.

Theorem~\ref{stabb} quantifies the corresponding rigidity.
Let $\Omega\subset\mathbb B^n$ be measurable with $0<\tau(\Omega)<\infty$, and let
$g\in \mathbf B_\alpha^2\setminus\{0\}$ be log-$\mathcal M$-subharmonic.
Writing $\delta(g;\Omega)$ for the deficit in the sharp concentration inequality, we prove a
power-law stability estimate
\[
\inf_{\substack{|c|=\|g\|_{2,\alpha}\\ |a|<1}}
\frac{\|g-cg_a\|_{2,\alpha}}{\|g\|_{2,\alpha}}
\;\lesssim\;
\Bigl(C(\tau(\Omega))\,\delta(g;\Omega)\Bigr)^{\frac{1}{n+2}},
\]
where $C(\tau(\Omega))=(1-\theta(\tau(\Omega)))^{-1}$ is an explicit amplification factor depending
only on the hyperbolic size of $\Omega$. In particular, in the planar case $n=2$ we obtain the
exponent $1/4$ (Theorem~\ref{stabb_n2_beta}) by the general method.
Section~\ref{ssec:sharpness-n2} then proves that this planar exponent is not
sharp: for positive real-analytic log-subharmonic functions on the disk one has
the global sharp estimate with exponent $1/2$,
Theorem~\ref{thm:global-half-from-green-absorption}. The proof uses the
real-analytic Riesz decomposition of $\log g$ and a perturbative bathtub
analysis around the centered extremal; these ingredients are not available in
the same form in the higher-dimensional log-$\mathcal M$ setting.

\subsection{Gaussian analogues in Fock space}

Beyond the hyperbolic ball, we prove stability analogues in Gaussian phase space.
For real-analytic log-subharmonic functions in the complex Fock space we obtain an inequality of
the form
\[
\min_{\substack{c=\|F\|_{\Fock}\\ z_0\in\mathbb C}}
\frac{\|F-cg_{z_0}\|_{\Fock}}{\|F\|_{\Fock}}
\;\lesssim\;
\bigl(e^{|\Omega|}\,\delta(F;\Omega)\bigr)^{1/4},
\]
and we extend this to $\mathbb R^m$ with exponent $1/(m+2)$.
The proofs follow the rearrangement-and-peak-control strategy, combined with Gaussian mean-value
estimates for log-subharmonic functions (Lemma~\ref{ulqin} and Lemma~\ref{ulqin2024}), in the spirit
of \cite{GomezGuerraRamosTilli2024}.
\begin{numrem}[Background and related results]
The terminology ``Faber--Krahn type inequality'' used in this paper is
motivated by the classical principle that balls are extremal for spectral,
isoperimetric, and concentration-type problems. In spaces of constant
negative curvature, sharp isoperimetric inequalities go back to Schmidt's
work on hyperbolic space \cite{14}. Quantitative stability versions of
hyperbolic isoperimetric inequalities were later studied, for example, by
B\"ogelein, Duzaar and Scheven \cite{cvpde}. Although the inequalities
considered here are functional concentration inequalities rather than
perimeter inequalities, the same geometric principle is present: extremal
sets are hyperbolic balls, and extremal functions are generated from a
canonical profile by the symmetry group.

The proof also relies on rearrangement ideas, such as the
Hardy--Littlewood principle, decreasing rearrangements, and comparison of
superlevel sets. Standard background on symmetrization and rearrangement
methods may be found in Baernstein's monograph \cite{baernstein}; for
general analytic inequalities and functional-analytic background we also
refer to Lieb and Loss \cite{LiebLoss}. In the Gaussian and Fock-space
parts, the relevant rearrangement estimates are closely related to
monotonicity principles for subharmonic functions. In particular, the
differential inequalities for decreasing rearrangements used here are in
the spirit of the monotonicity theorem of Kulikov, Nicola,
Ortega-Cerd\`a and Tilli \cite{KulikovNicolaOrtegaCerdaTilli2025}. These
principles appear, for instance, in inequalities of the form
\[
        (u^*)'(s)+u^*(s)\ge 0
\]
in the planar Gaussian setting, and in their higher-dimensional analogues.

Finally, the Bergman-space part of the paper is connected with recent
stability results for sharp concentration inequalities in holomorphic
function spaces. Besides the uniform stability results from
\cite{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025}, related sharp stability phenomena
for convex functionals on weighted Bergman spaces were studied by
Melentijevi\'c \cite{Melentijevic}. These results form part of the
holomorphic background for the contrast emphasized in the present work: the
planar log-subharmonic class is much larger than the holomorphic class, but
real analyticity and the Riesz decomposition still impose enough rigidity to
recover the same sharp deficit exponent \(1/2\).
\end{numrem}
\subsection{Ideas of the proof and organization}

Across the different settings, the arguments share three main ingredients.
First, we reduce arbitrary sets to extremal (superlevel) sets via Hardy--Littlewood rearrangement.
Second, we convert deficit control into a quantitative bound on the peak value of a normalized
density (hyperbolic: $u=\Phi_n^\alpha|g|^2$, Gaussian: $u=|F|^2e^{-\pi|\,\cdot\,|^2}$), using a
clipped comparison functional and, in higher dimension, differential inequalities for decreasing
rearrangements. Third, we pass from peak control to distance-to-extremals by a quantitative
reproducing-kernel/RKHS estimate (cf.\ \cite{kulikov2022}).

The paper is organized as follows. In Section~2 we treat the vector-valued holomorphic Bergman case
in the unit disk, proving stability with the sharp exponent $1/2$ and deriving modulus and
square-sum corollaries, following the approach of \cite{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025}.
In Section~3 we establish stability for log-subharmonic and log-$\mathcal M$-subharmonic data in
hyperbolic geometry, proving Theorem~\ref{stabb}, the explicit planar $1/4$
estimate, and then the sharp planar $1/2$ theorem. Section~4 develops the
Fock-space analogues in $\mathbb C$ and $\mathbb R^m$.

\section{Stability of vector-valued holomorphic functions}

Fix $\alpha>-1$. We write
\[
dA_\alpha(z):=(1-|z|^2)^\alpha\,dz,
\qquad
d\mu(z):=(1-|z|^2)^{-2}\,dz,
\qquad
s:=\mu(\Omega)\in(0,\infty).
\]
Let $\mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D)$ be the weighted Bergman space with norm
$\|f\|_{\Berg}^2:=\int_\D |f|^2\,dA_\alpha$.
For $\omega\in\D$, let the normalized Bergman extremals be
\[
\mathbf f_{\omega}(z)
=
\sqrt{\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}}\,
\frac{(1-|\omega|^2)^{\frac{\alpha+2}{2}}}{(1-\overline{\omega}z)^{\alpha+2}},
\qquad \|\mathbf f_\omega\|_{\Berg}=1.
\]
We also recall the sharp concentration profile (for hyperbolic balls of $\mu$-measure $s$)
\[
\theta_\alpha(s):=1-\Bigl(1+\frac{s}{\pi}\Bigr)^{-(\alpha+1)}.
\]
Given $f\in\mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D)$ and $\Omega\subset\D$ with $\mu(\Omega)=s$, define the deficit
\begin{equation}\label{eq:deficit-scalar}
\delta(f;\Omega,\alpha)
:=
1-\frac{\int_\Omega |f(z)|^2\,dA_\alpha(z)}{\|f\|_{\Berg}^2\,\theta_\alpha(s)}
\in[0,1].
\end{equation}
(For the vector-valued extension below, we replace $|f|^2$ by $\|F\|_{\C^N}^2$.)

\medskip

We shall use the following explicit stability result, proved in our setting in
\cite[Theorem~1.2]{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025}.

\begin{numthm}[Bergman stability]\label{thm:BergmanStability}
Let \(\alpha>-1\), let \(\Omega\subset\D\) satisfy \(\mu(\Omega)=s>0\), and let
\(f\in \mathbf B_\alpha^2(\mathbb{D})\). Then there exists an absolute, computable constant
\(C>0\) such that
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:BergmanStabilityFunction}
\inf_{\substack{|c|=\|f\|_{\mathbf B_\alpha^2}\\ \omega\in\D}}
\frac{\|f-c\,\mathbf{f}_{\omega}\|_{\mathbf B_\alpha^2}}{\|f\|_{\mathbf B_\alpha^2}}
\le
M(s,\alpha)\,\delta(f;\Omega,\alpha)^{1/2},
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:def-Msa}
M(s,\alpha)
:=
C\left(
1+\frac{\alpha+2}{\alpha+1}
\left[
\left(1+\frac{s}{\pi}\right)^{\alpha+1}-1
\right]
\right)^{1/2},
\end{equation}
and where \(\mathbf{f}_{\omega}\) is the normalized reproducing kernel of
\(\mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D)\), given explicitly by
\[
\mathbf{f}_{\omega}(z)
=
\sqrt{\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}}\,
\frac{(1-|\omega|^2)^{\frac{\alpha+2}{2}}}{(1-\overline{\omega}z)^{\alpha+2}}.
\]

Moreover, there exists an explicit constant \(K(s,\alpha)>0\) such that
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:BergmanStabilitySet}
\mcalA_{\D}(\Omega)
\le
K(s,\alpha)\,\delta(f;\Omega,\alpha)^{1/2}.
\end{equation}
\end{numthm}


\medskip

\subsection*{Vector-valued extension (kernel times a vector)}

For $N\in\N$, define the vector-valued Bergman space
$\mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D;\C^N)$ with norm
$\|F\|_{\Berg}^2:=\int_\D \|F(z)\|_{\C^N}^2\,dA_\alpha(z)$.
Its reproducing kernel is the scalar Bergman kernel times $I_N$,
so the extremals are exactly the functions $z\mapsto \mathbf f_\omega(z)\,v$ with $\omega\in\D$
and $v\in\C^N$.

Define the vector deficit (depending only on $\|F\|^2$) by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:deficit-vector}
\delta(F;\Omega,\alpha)
:=
1-\frac{\int_\Omega \|F(z)\|_{\C^N}^2\,dA_\alpha(z)}{\|F\|_{\Berg}^2\,\theta_\alpha(s)}.
\end{equation}

\begin{numthm}[Vector-valued stability: same constants]\label{lem:vector-stability-same-constants}
For every $N\in\N$, every $F\in \mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D;\C^N)$, and every $\Omega\subset\D$ with $\mu(\Omega)=s$,
\begin{align}
\inf_{\substack{v\in\C^N,\ \omega\in\D\\ \|v\|_{\C^N}=\|F\|_{\Berg}}}
\frac{\|F-\mathbf f_\omega\,v\|_{\Berg}}{\|F\|_{\Berg}}
&\le \mathsf M(s,\alpha)\,\delta(F;\Omega,\alpha)^{1/2},\label{eq:vector-fun-stab}\\
\mathcal A_{\D}(\Omega)
&\le \mathsf K(s,\alpha)\,\delta(F;\Omega,\alpha)^{1/2}.
\label{eq:vector-set-stab}
\end{align}
In particular, the constants are independent of $N$.
\end{numthm}
\begin{proof}
By homogeneity we may assume $F\not\equiv 0$ and normalize
\[
\|F\|_{\Berg}=1.
\]
All estimates below are invariant under the scaling $F\mapsto \lambda F$, and we rescale at the end.

Define
\[
u_F(z):=\|F(z)\|_{\C^N}^2(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2},\qquad
\rho_F(t):=\mu(\{z\in\D:\ u_F(z)>t\}),
\]
and set
\[
a_0^2:=\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\max_{z\in\D}u_F(z)\in(0,1],\qquad
\delta_0^2:=1-a_0^2.
\]
Choose $\omega\in\D$ such that $u_F(\omega)=\max_\D u_F$. Since
\[
K(\omega,\omega)=\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}(1-|\omega|^2)^{-(\alpha+2)},
\]
we obtain
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Delta_equals_delta0_vector}
\|F(\omega)\|_{\C^N}^2\,K(\omega,\omega)^{-1}
=\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\,u_F(\omega)=a_0^2,
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:RKHSdef_at_omega}
\Delta_F(\omega)
:=1-\|F(\omega)\|_{\C^N}^2K(\omega,\omega)^{-1}
=1-a_0^2
=\delta_0^2.
\end{equation}

We first establish the set estimate \eqref{eq:vector-set-stab}. The scalar set-stability argument depends only on the distribution function of the nonnegative quantity
\[
u(z)=|f(z)|^2(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2}
\]
and on the associated rearrangement inequalities over sets of fixed $\mu$-measure. In the present vector-valued setting, the concentration functional and the deficit depend on $F$ only through the scalar function $u_F$. Thus the same rearrangement argument applies verbatim with $u$ replaced by $u_F$, and yields
\[
\mathcal A_{\D}(\Omega)\le \mathsf K(s,\alpha)\,\delta(F;\Omega,\alpha)^{1/2}.
\]
In particular, the constant $\mathsf K(s,\alpha)$ is independent of $N$.

Next we control the peak value of $u_F$ in terms of the deficit. By Lemma~\ref{lemma:BergmanFunctionStabilityBeforeRKHSVector},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:preRKHS_in_proof}
1-a_0^2 \le M_\alpha(s)\,\delta(F;\Omega,\alpha).
\end{equation}
The proof of that lemma is the same as in the scalar case, with
\[
u(z)=|f(z)|^2(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2}
\]
replaced by
\[
u_F(z)=\|F(z)\|_{\C^N}^2(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2}.
\]
It uses only the superlevel-set estimate from Lemma~\ref{lemma:BergmanSuperLevelSetsEstimateVector}, the rearrangement-gap lower bound from Lemma~\ref{lemma:BergmanLowerBoundDifferenceVector}, and the ratio-monotonicity coming from the log-subharmonicity of $u_F$; the latter holds because $\log\|F\|_{\C^N}$ is subharmonic. Hence the function $M_\alpha(s)$ is also dimension-free.

We now pass from the pointwise deficit at $\omega$ to a norm-distance estimate. Applying Lemma~\ref{lemma:RKHSQuantitativeVector} to the RKHS $\mcalH=\mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D;\C^N)$ at the point $\omega$, and using \eqref{eq:RKHSdef_at_omega}, we find a unit vector $e\in\C^N$ and a scalar $c\in\C$ with $|c|=\|F\|_{\Berg}=1$ such that
\[
\|F-c\,\mathbf f_\omega e\|_{\Berg}
\le \sqrt2\,\Delta_F(\omega)^{1/2}
=\sqrt2\,(1-a_0^2)^{1/2}.
\]
Combining this with \eqref{eq:preRKHS_in_proof} gives
\[
\inf_{\substack{\|v\|_{\C^N}=1\\ \omega\in\D}}
\|F-\mathbf f_\omega v\|_{\Berg}
\le \sqrt2\,M_\alpha(s)^{1/2}\,\delta(F;\Omega,\alpha)^{1/2}.
\]
Thus, with
\[
\mathsf M(s,\alpha):=\sqrt2\,M_\alpha(s)^{1/2},
\]
we obtain \eqref{eq:vector-fun-stab} in the normalized case $\|F\|_{\Berg}=1$.

Finally, rescaling from $F/\|F\|_{\Berg}$ back to $F$ yields the stated infimum condition
\[
\|v\|_{\C^N}=\|F\|_{\Berg},
\]
and proves \eqref{eq:vector-fun-stab} in full generality. Since all constants involved come from Lemmas
\ref{lemma:BergmanSuperLevelSetsEstimateVector},
\ref{lemma:BergmanLowerBoundDifferenceVector},
\ref{lemma:BergmanFunctionStabilityBeforeRKHSVector}, and
\ref{lemma:RKHSQuantitativeVector},
all of which are dimension-free, the constants $\mathsf M(s,\alpha)$ and $\mathsf K(s,\alpha)$ are independent of $N$.
\end{proof}

\begin{numcor}\label{cor:modulus_and_logsubharmonic_stability_selfcontained}
Fix $\alpha>-1$ and $\Omega\subset\D$ with $\mu(\Omega)=s>0$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{(A) Modulus stability (scalar).}
For every $f\in \mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:modulus_stability_selfcontained}
\inf_{\substack{c>0\\ \omega\in\D}}
\frac{\big\||f|-c\,|\mathbf f_{\omega}|\big\|_{\Berg}}{\|f\|_{\Berg}}
\;\le\;
\mathsf M(s,\alpha)\,\delta(f;\Omega,\alpha)^{1/2}.
\end{equation}
Moreover, $\mathcal A_{\D}(\Omega)\le \mathsf K(s,\alpha)\,\delta(f;\Omega,\alpha)^{1/2}$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{(B) Square-sum stability (Hilbert envelope).}
Let $N\in\N$, $f_1,\dots,f_N\in \mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D)$, and set
\[
G(z):=\Big(\sum_{j=1}^N |f_j(z)|^2\Big)^{1/2}.
\]
Let $F:=(f_1,\dots,f_N)\in \mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D;\C^N)$, so that $G(z)=\|F(z)\|_{\C^N}$.
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:squaresum_stability_selfcontained}
\inf_{\substack{c>0\\ \omega\in\D}}
\frac{\|G-c\,|\mathbf f_{\omega}|\|_{\Berg}}{\|G\|_{\Berg}}
\;\le\;
\mathsf M(s,\alpha)\,\delta(F;\Omega,\alpha)^{1/2},
\end{equation}
and $\mathcal A_{\D}(\Omega)\le \mathsf K(s,\alpha)\,\delta(F;\Omega,\alpha)^{1/2}$.

\end{numcor}
\begin{proof}
\textbf{(A)} For any $c\in\C$ and $\omega\in\D$ we have the pointwise inequality
\[
\big||f(z)|-|c|\,|\mathbf f_\omega(z)|\big|
\le |f(z)-c\,\mathbf f_\omega(z)|.
\]
Integrating against $dA_\alpha$ yields
$\||f|-|c||\mathbf f_\omega|\|_{\Berg}\le \|f-c\mathbf f_\omega\|_{\Berg}$.
Taking the infimum over $c,\omega$ and using \eqref{eqn:BergmanStabilityFunction} gives
\eqref{eq:modulus_stability_selfcontained}. The set bound is \eqref{eqn:BergmanStabilitySet}.

\smallskip
\textbf{(B)} Let $F=(f_1,\dots,f_N)$ and $G(z)=\|F(z)\|_{\C^N}$.
Fix $\omega\in\D$, $c>0$ and a unit vector $e\in\C^N$.
By the reverse triangle inequality in $\C^N$,
\[
\big|\|F(z)\|_{\C^N}-\|c\,\mathbf f_\omega(z)\,e\|_{\C^N}\big|
\le \|F(z)-c\,\mathbf f_\omega(z)\,e\|_{\C^N}.
\]
Since $\|c\,\mathbf f_\omega(z)\,e\|=c\,|\mathbf f_\omega(z)|$, squaring and integrating gives
\[
\|G-c|\mathbf f_\omega|\|_{\Berg}
\le \|F-c\,\mathbf f_\omega e\|_{\mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D;\C^N)}.
\]
Taking infima over $c>0$, $\omega\in\D$ and $\|e\|=1$, and applying Lemma~\ref{lem:vector-stability-same-constants},
we obtain \eqref{eq:squaresum_stability_selfcontained} and the corresponding set estimate.

\end{proof}

\begin{numlemma}[Quantitative RKHS estimate, vector-valued]\label{lemma:RKHSQuantitativeVector}
Let $\mcalH$ be a reproducing kernel Hilbert space of $\mathbb{C}^N$-valued functions on $X$
whose reproducing kernel is $K(\cdot,\cdot)I_N$, and let $F\in \mcalH$.
For $x\in X$ define
\[
\Delta_F(x):=\frac{\|F\|_{\mcalH}^2-\|F(x)\|_{\mathbb{C}^N}^2\,K(x,x)^{-1}}{\|F\|_{\mcalH}^2},
\qquad\text{so that}\qquad
\|F(x)\|_{\mathbb{C}^N}^2\,K(x,x)^{-1}\le \|F\|_{\mcalH}^2 .
\]
Then for every $x\in X$ there exist $c\in\mathbb{C}$ and a unit vector $e\in\mathbb{C}^N$ with
$|c|=\|F\|_{\mcalH}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:QuantitativeRKHSVector}
\frac{\|F-c f_x e\|_{\mcalH}}{\|F\|_{\mcalH}}
\le \sqrt2\,\Delta_F(x)^{1/2},
\end{equation}
where $f_x = K(x,x)^{-1/2}K_x$ is the normalized kernel in the scalar space.
\end{numlemma}

\begin{proof}
Fix $x\in X$. If $F(x)\neq 0$, choose $e:=F(x)/\|F(x)\|_{\mathbb{C}^N}$ and
$c:=\|F\|_{\mcalH}$ (so $|c|=\|F\|_{\mcalH}$). Using the reproducing property,
\[
\langle F, f_x e\rangle_{\mcalH}
=\frac{\langle F(x),e\rangle_{\mathbb{C}^N}}{\sqrt{K(x,x)}}
=\frac{\|F(x)\|_{\mathbb{C}^N}}{\sqrt{K(x,x)}}\in\mathbb{R}_+ .
\]
Hence
\begin{align*}
\|F-c f_x e\|_{\mcalH}^2
&=\|F\|_{\mcalH}^2+|c|^2\|f_x e\|_{\mcalH}^2-2\Re\langle F, c f_x e\rangle_{\mcalH}\\
&=2\|F\|_{\mcalH}^2-2\|F\|_{\mcalH}\frac{\|F(x)\|_{\mathbb{C}^N}}{\sqrt{K(x,x)}}\\
&\le 2\|F\|_{\mcalH}^2-2\frac{\|F(x)\|_{\mathbb{C}^N}^2}{K(x,x)}
=2\|F\|_{\mcalH}^2\,\Delta_F(x),
\end{align*}
where in the inequality we used $\|F(x)\|_{\mathbb{C}^N}/\sqrt{K(x,x)}\le \|F\|_{\mcalH}$.
This gives \eqref{eqn:QuantitativeRKHSVector}.

If $F(x)=0$, then $\Delta_F(x)=1$ and taking any unit $e$ and $c=\|F\|_{\mcalH}$
yields $\|F-c f_x e\|_{\mcalH}^2=\|F\|_{\mcalH}^2+|c|^2=2\|F\|_{\mcalH}^2$,
so \eqref{eqn:QuantitativeRKHSVector} holds as well.
\end{proof}

\begin{numlemma}[Super-level set estimate for vector-valued Bergman data]\label{lemma:BergmanSuperLevelSetsEstimateVector}
Let $\alpha>-1$ and let $F\in \mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D;\C^N)$ satisfy $\|F\|_{\mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D;\C^N)}=1$.
Define
\[
u_F(z):=\|F(z)\|_{\C^N}^2(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2},\qquad
\rho_F(t):=\mu(\{z\in\D:\ u_F(z)>t\}),
\]
and set
\[
a_0^2:=\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\max_{z\in\D}u_F(z)\in(0,1],\qquad \delta_0^2:=1-a_0^2,
\qquad T:=\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}a_0^2.
\]
Then there exists $\widetilde c_0\in(0,1)$ such that for every $c_0\ge \widetilde c_0$
there exist $C_0\in(c_0,1)$ and $C>0$ with the following property:
if $a_0^2\ge C_0$, then for every
\[
t\in\Big(\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}c_0,\ \frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}a_0^2\Big)
\]
one has
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:BergmanSuperLevelSetsEstimateVector}
\rho_F(t)\le \pi\Big(\frac{1+K_0(1-a_0^2)}{a_0^2}\Big)
\Big(\Big(\frac{\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}a_0^2}{t}\Big)^{\frac1{\alpha+2}}-1\Big),
\qquad K_0=\frac{C}{c_0^3}.
\end{equation}
Moreover, the constants can be chosen independently of $N$.
\end{numlemma}

\begin{proof}
The proof follows the scalar argument of Lemma~3.1 \cite{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025}
(and the corresponding level-set analysis in \cite{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025}),
with one additional estimate for the components orthogonal to the maximizer direction.
We indicate the modifications and keep the rest identical.

Let $z_\ast$ maximize $u_F$. By composing with a disk automorphism and multiplying by a unitary matrix
(and a unimodular scalar), we may assume (see \cite[Lemma~3.1]{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025}) that
\[
F(0)=\sqrt{T}\,e_1,\qquad u_F(0)=\max_\D u_F,\qquad \langle F'(0),F(0)\rangle_{\C^N}=0,
\]
so in particular the first component $f:=\langle F,e_1\rangle$ satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:first-component-vanish}
f(0)=\sqrt{T},\qquad f'(0)=0.
\end{equation}
Decompose
\[
F(z)= f(z)e_1 + H(z),\qquad H(z)\in e_1^\perp,\quad \langle H(z),e_1\rangle=0,\quad H(0)=0,
\]
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:decomp-norm}
\|F(z)\|_{\C^N}^2 = |f(z)|^2 + \|H(z)\|_{\C^N}^2,\qquad z\in\D.
\end{equation}

Write
\[
\frac{f(z)}{\sqrt{T}} = 1+R(z).
\]
By \eqref{eq:first-component-vanish}, the Taylor series of $R$ starts at order $\ge2$,
exactly as in the scalar proof. Writing $f(z)=\sum_{k\ge 0} a_k z^k/\sqrt{c_k}$
in the usual orthonormal Bergman basis (for the scalar space),
one has $a_1=0$ and
\[
\sum_{k\ge2}|a_k|^2 \le \sum_{k\ge1}|a_k|^2 \le 1-a_0^2=\delta_0^2.
\]
Thus, setting $\delta^2:=\delta_0^2/T$, Cauchy--Schwarz yields the same bounds as in the scalar proof:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:BoundsR-vector}
|R(z)|\le \delta\, g_1(|z|)^{1/2},\qquad
|R'(z)|\le \delta\, g_2(|z|)^{1/2},\qquad
|R''(z)|\le \delta\, g_3(|z|)^{1/2},
\end{equation}
with the same explicit functions $g_1,g_2,g_3$ as in Lemma~3.1 in \cite{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025}.

Let $h(z):=2\Re R(z)$; then $h$ is harmonic and \eqref{eq:BoundsR-vector} implies
the same bounds as \cite[eq.~3.4]{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025} in the scalar proof.

It remains only to estimate the orthogonal component $H$. Expand each component of $H$ in the same orthonormal basis. Since $H(0)=0$,
all constant coefficients vanish. Moreover,
\[
\|H\|_{\mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D;\C^N)}^2 \le \|F\|^2 - \|f\|^2 \le 1-a_0^2=\delta_0^2.
\]
Hence, by Cauchy--Schwarz and the diagonal kernel identity
\[
\sum_{k\ge0}\frac{|z|^{2k}}{c_k}=K(z,z)=\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}(1-|z|^2)^{-(\alpha+2)},
\]
we obtain the pointwise estimate
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H-bound}
\frac{\|H(z)\|_{\C^N}^2}{T}
\le
\frac{\delta_0^2}{T}\sum_{k\ge1}\frac{|z|^{2k}}{c_k}
=
\delta^2\,\widehat g_1(|z|),
\qquad
\widehat g_1(r):=\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}\big[(1-r^2)^{-(\alpha+2)}-1\big].
\end{equation}
Compared to the scalar proof, $\widehat g_1$ is the same kernel series but without subtracting
the $k=1$ term; this is exactly where the possible linear terms of $H$ are absorbed.

Now fix $z=re^{i\theta}$ and assume $u_F(z)>t$. Using \eqref{eq:decomp-norm} we get
\[
\frac{t}{T}(1-r^2)^{-(\alpha+2)}
<
\frac{|f(z)|^2+\|H(z)\|^2}{T}
=
|1+R(z)|^2+\frac{\|H(z)\|^2}{T}
=
1+h(z)+|R(z)|^2+\frac{\|H(z)\|^2}{T}.
\]
Using \eqref{eq:BoundsR-vector} to bound $|R(z)|^2\le \delta^2 g_1(r)$ and then \eqref{eq:H-bound}, we obtain
\begin{equation}\label{eq:boundary-condition-vector}
\frac{t}{T}(1-r^2)^{-(\alpha+2)}
<
1+\delta^2\big(g_1(r)+\widehat g_1(r)\big)+h(re^{i\theta}).
\end{equation}
Define the modified radial term
\[
g_1^\ast(r):=g_1(r)+\widehat g_1(r)
=\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}\big[(1-r^2)^{-(\alpha+2)}-1-(\alpha+2)r^2\big]
+\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}\big[(1-r^2)^{-(\alpha+2)}-1\big],
\]
and repeat the scalar construction with
\[
g_\theta(r,\sigma):=\frac{t}{T}(1-r^2)^{-(\alpha+2)}-\delta^2 g_1^\ast(r)-\sigma h(re^{i\theta}).
\]
Then \eqref{eq:boundary-condition-vector} implies $g_\theta(r,1)<1$ and the star-shaped sets $E_\sigma$
are defined exactly as in the scalar proof.

All subsequent steps---boundedness and star-shapedness of $E_\sigma$ for $t>\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}c_0$,
the Taylor expansion of $F(\sigma)=\mu(E_\sigma)$, the estimates on $r_\sigma',r_\sigma''$,
the comparison of $F(1)$ and $F(0)$, and the final estimate for $\mu(E_1)$---use only:
(i) the harmonicity bounds for $h$ coming from \eqref{eq:BoundsR-vector}, and
(ii) the fact that the radial error term is of size $\delta^2$ times an explicit function of $r$.
Replacing $g_1$ by the larger $g_1^\ast$ changes only universal constants (and yields the same structure
$K_0=C/c_0^3$ after the same bookkeeping), while remaining independent of $N$.

Therefore the scalar argument yields \eqref{eqn:BergmanSuperLevelSetsEstimateVector}.
\end{proof}
\begin{numrem}
The threshold $\widetilde c_0$ arises from a purely one-dimensional inequality comparing explicit bounds
on $r_0$ (as in the scalar proof); the same comparison works here after enlarging the radial term to $g_1^\ast$.
Thus one may keep the same qualitative statement (and in practice the same numerical value, up to re-optimizing constants).
Since the paper does not depend on optimizing $\widetilde c_0$, we do not track the best value.
\end{numrem}

\begin{numlemma}[Lower bound on the rearrangement gap: vector-valued case]\label{lemma:BergmanLowerBoundDifferenceVector}
Let $\alpha>-1$ and let $F\in \mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D;\C^N)$ with $\|F\|_{\mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D;\C^N)}=1$.
Set
\[
u_F(z):=\|F(z)\|_{\C^N}^2(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2},\qquad
a_0^2:=\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\max_{\D}u_F,\qquad \delta_0^2:=1-a_0^2,
\]
and let $u_F^*$ be the decreasing rearrangement of $u_F$ with respect to $\mu$.
Let $v^*(s):=\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}\Big(1+\frac{s}{\pi}\Big)^{-(\alpha+2)}$.
Let $s^*>0$ be the minimal solution of $u_F^*(s)=v^*(s)$.

Assume Lemma~\ref{lemma:BergmanSuperLevelSetsEstimateVector} holds for $u_F$
(with constants $c_0,C_0,K_0$ as there).
Then there exists an absolute constant $C>0$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:BergmanLowerBoundDifferenceVector}
C\,\frac{\alpha+1}{\alpha+2}\,(1-a_0^2)
\;\le\;
\int_0^{s^*}\big(v^*(s)-u_F^*(s)\big)\,ds .
\end{equation}
Moreover, the constant $C$ can be chosen independently of $N$.
\end{numlemma}

\begin{proof}
This is the scalar proof with $u$ replaced by $u_F$ and $\rho$ replaced by $\rho_F$.
We sketch the two places where an input is used.

Let $t^*:=v^*(s^*)=u_F^*(s^*)$. First, one shows that
\[
t^*\le \frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}\,c
\]
for some absolute $c\in(0,1)$: if $t^*\le \frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}c_0$ we are done;
otherwise, apply Lemma~\ref{lemma:BergmanSuperLevelSetsEstimateVector} at $t=t^*$ and use
$\rho_F(t^*)=s^*=\pi\big((\frac{\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}}{t^*})^{\frac1{\alpha+2}}-1\big)$
to obtain the analogue of \cite[eq.~3.16]{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025} and hence a uniform
upper bound on $t^*$ (exactly as in your scalar argument).

Next, let $\omega(t)$ denote the right-hand side of the superlevel estimate from
Lemma~\ref{lemma:BergmanSuperLevelSetsEstimateVector}, and let $\nu$ be the inverse of $v^*$,
\[
\nu(t)=\pi\Big(\Big(\frac{\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}}{t}\Big)^{\frac1{\alpha+2}}-1\Big).
\]
Then the rearrangement identity and the inequality $\rho_F(t)\le \omega(t)$ on the admissible
range yield
\[
\int_0^{s^*}(v^*-u_F^*)\,ds
\;\ge\;
\int_{\tau_1}^{T}(\nu(t)-\omega(t))\,dt,
\qquad
T=\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}a_0^2,
\]
for a suitable $\tau_1$ (chosen as in the scalar proof so that $\tau_1>\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}c_0$).
Carrying out the same change of variables $t=\frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}r$ then gives the same
one-dimensional estimate in $r$, with the same elementary positivity argument for the first
term in the expansion (your $f_1(r)$ bound). This yields a lower bound of the form
\[
\int_0^{s^*}(v^*-u_F^*)\,ds \;\gtrsim\; \frac{\alpha+1}{\alpha+2}\,\delta_0^2,
\]
i.e. \eqref{eqn:BergmanLowerBoundDifferenceVector}.
All constants remain independent of $N$ because the only nontrivial input is the
dimension-free superlevel estimate (Lemma~\ref{lemma:BergmanSuperLevelSetsEstimateVector}).
\end{proof}


\begin{numlemma}[Pre-RKHS bound: vector-valued case]\label{lemma:BergmanFunctionStabilityBeforeRKHSVector}
Let $\alpha>-1$, $\Omega\subset\D$ with $s=\mu(\Omega)>0$, and let
$F\in \mathbf B_\alpha^2(\D;\C^N)$.
Define the deficit (depending only on $u_F=\|F\|^2(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2}$) by
\[
\delta(F;\Omega,\alpha)
:=
\frac{\|F\|^2\,\theta_\alpha(s)-\int_\Omega u_F\,d\mu}{\|F\|^2\,\theta_\alpha(s)},
\qquad
\theta_\alpha(s):=1-\Big(1+\frac{s}{\pi}\Big)^{-(\alpha+1)}.
\]
Then there exists a function $M_\alpha(s)$ (the same one as in the scalar proof) such that
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:BergmanFunctionStabilityBeforeRKHSVector}
1-a_0^2 \;\le\; M_\alpha(s)\,\delta(F;\Omega,\alpha),
\end{equation}
where \(a_0^2=\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\max_\D u_F\).
Moreover, $M_\alpha(s)$ is independent of $N$.

One may take for instance
\[
M_\alpha(s)=C\Big(1+\frac{\alpha+2}{\alpha+1}\Big[\Big(1+\frac{s}{\pi}\Big)^{\alpha+1}-1\Big]\Big),
\]
with $C>0$ an absolute constant.
\end{numlemma}

\begin{proof}
This is again the scalar proof with $u$ replaced by $u_F$.
Write $s_0:=\mu(\Omega)$ and let $A:=\{u_F>u_F^*(s_0)\}$ be the superlevel set of $u_F$ of measure $s_0$.
Set $\delta_{s_0}:=\delta(F;A,\alpha)\le \delta(F;\Omega,\alpha)$ and define
\[
\varepsilon := \int_0^{s_0}(v^*(s)-u_F^*(s))\,ds
=\delta_{s_0}\int_0^{s_0}v^*(s)\,ds.
\]

A key input is the monotonicity of the ratio
\[
r(s):=\frac{u_F^*(s)}{v^*(s)}.
\]
Since $F$ is holomorphic, $\log\|F\|_{\C^N}$ is subharmonic (with $\log 0=-\infty$), hence
$\|F\|_{\C^N}^2$ is log--subharmonic. Therefore monotonicity extends
verbatim to the $\C^N$--valued setting; see \cite{RamosTilli,GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025}.
From here, the two case splits ($s_0>s^*$ and $s_0<s^*$) proceed verbatim, yielding the analogues
of \cite[eq.3.21-eq.3.24]{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025}, where the lower bound
\eqref{eqn:BergmanLowerBoundDifferenceVector} is used in place of \cite[Lemma~3.2]{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025}.
Combining those estimates gives \eqref{eqn:BergmanFunctionStabilityBeforeRKHSVector} with
\[
M_\alpha(s)=C\Big(1+\frac{\alpha+2}{\alpha+1}\big[(1+s/\pi)^{\alpha+1}-1\big]\Big),
\]
exactly as in the scalar Step~III.
All constants are independent of $N$ because (i) the ratio-monotonicity depends only on
log-subharmonicity of $u_F$, and (ii) Lemma~\ref{lemma:BergmanLowerBoundDifferenceVector}
is dimension-free.
\end{proof}

\begin{numrem}
The following examples and propositions anticipate the sharp half-stability
theorem proved below. They isolate model mechanisms which later reappear in
the full real-analytic proof: radial monotonicity, Hilbert-valued holomorphic
factorization, and bathtub control for special superlevel geometries. In the
global argument these separate mechanisms are replaced by the Riesz
decomposition of $\log g$ and the perturbative bathtub analysis around the
extremal disk.
\end{numrem}
\begin{numexamp}\label{lem:l2factorization_radial}
Let $a\in(0,1)$. Define holomorphic functions $a_k:\mathbb D\to\mathbb C$ by
\[
a_{n+1}(z)\coloneqq \sqrt{n+1}\,a^{n/2}\,z^{n},\qquad n=0,1,2,\dots .
\]
Then for every $z\in\mathbb D$ we have
\[
\frac{1}{1-a|z|^{2}}=\sqrt{\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\lvert a_k(z)\rvert^{2}}.
\]
In particular, the map $A:\mathbb D\to \ell^{2}$ given by 
\[
A(z)=(a_k(z))_{k\ge1}
\]
is holomorphic and satisfies
\[
\|A(z)\|_{\ell^{2}}=\frac{1}{1-a|z|^{2}}
\qquad \text{for all } z\in\mathbb D.
\]
\end{numexamp}

\begin{proof}
For $|x|<1$ we have the power series identity
\[
\frac{1}{(1-x)^2}=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(n+1)x^{n}.
\]
Substituting $x=a|z|^{2}$ (which satisfies $|x|<1$ for $z\in\mathbb D$) gives
\[
\frac{1}{(1-a|z|^{2})^{2}}
=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(n+1)a^{n}|z|^{2n}.
\]
By definition,
\[
|a_{n+1}(z)|^{2}=(n+1)a^{n}|z|^{2n},
\]
and therefore
\[
\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}|a_k(z)|^{2}
=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(n+1)a^{n}|z|^{2n}
=\frac{1}{(1-a|z|^{2})^{2}}.
\]
Taking square roots completes the proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{numexamp}[Hilbert--valued holomorphic factorization for radial functions]\label{thm:radial_hilbert_factorization}
Let $u:\mathbb D\to(0,\infty)$ be radial, $u(z)=U(|z|)$, and assume that the function
\[
F(r)\coloneqq U(r)^2
\]
admits a (real-analytic) power series expansion in $r^2$ with \emph{nonnegative} coefficients,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:radial_nonneg_series}
F(r)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n\,r^{2n},\qquad b_n\ge 0,\qquad 0\le r<1.
\end{equation}
Then there exist holomorphic functions $a_k:\mathbb D\to\mathbb C$ such that for every $z\in\mathbb D$,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:radial_l2_factor}
u(z)=\Big(\sum_{k=0}^\infty |a_k(z)|^2\Big)^{1/2}.
\end{equation}
More precisely, one may take
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:radial_l2_factor_explicit}
a_n(z)\coloneqq \sqrt{b_n}\,z^n,\qquad n=0,1,2,\dots,
\end{equation}
so that $\sum_{n\ge 0}|a_n(z)|^2=\sum_{n\ge 0} b_n |z|^{2n}=F(|z|)=u(z)^2$.

Moreover, defining the partial sums
\[
u_N(z)\coloneqq \Big(\sum_{n=0}^N |a_n(z)|^2\Big)^{1/2},
\]
we have $u_N(z)\uparrow u(z)$ pointwise for all $z\in\mathbb D$, and the convergence is uniform on compact subsets of $\mathbb D$.

\smallskip
If, in addition, $u$ is $C^2$ on $\mathbb D$ and each $a_n$ is holomorphic, then the right-hand side of \eqref{eqn:radial_l2_factor} is log-subharmonic; in particular, any $u$ represented as in \eqref{eqn:radial_l2_factor} is log-subharmonic.
\end{numexamp}

\begin{proof}
Assumption \eqref{eqn:radial_nonneg_series} implies $F(|z|)=\sum_{n\ge 0} b_n |z|^{2n}$ for all $z\in\mathbb D$.
With $a_n$ as in \eqref{eqn:radial_l2_factor_explicit}, each $a_n$ is holomorphic and
\[
\sum_{n=0}^\infty |a_n(z)|^2=\sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n |z|^{2n}=F(|z|)=u(z)^2,
\]
which gives \eqref{eqn:radial_l2_factor}. Monotone convergence of $u_N$ is immediate since $b_n\ge 0$.
Uniform convergence on compacta follows from uniform convergence of the power series for $F(r)$ on $[0,r_0]$ for any $r_0<1$.

For the final claim, note that $z\mapsto \sum_{n=0}^N |a_n(z)|^2$ is subharmonic as a finite sum of squared moduli of holomorphic functions, hence $\log \sum_{n=0}^N |a_n(z)|^2$ is subharmonic wherever the sum is positive. Passing to $N\to\infty$ preserves log-subharmonicity under locally uniform limits (or via standard monotone limit arguments), yielding that $u$ is log-subharmonic.
\end{proof}

\begin{numlemma}[Centered-disk radial stability]
\label{lem:centered_disk_radial}
Let $\alpha>-1$ and let $g:\D\to[0,\infty)$ be radial and nondecreasing in $|z|$.
Assume
\[
\|g\|_{2,\alpha}^2=\int_{\D} g(z)^2\,dA_\alpha(z)=1.
\]
Fix $r\in(0,1)$ and write
\[
W:=1-(1-r^2)^{\alpha+1},
\qquad
S:=(1-r^2)^{\alpha+1}.
\]
Define the centered-disk deficit
\[
\delta_{r,\alpha}(g)
:=1-\frac{1}{W}\int_{|z|<r} g(z)^2\,dA_\alpha(z)\in[0,1].
\]
Then
\[
\|g-1\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le \frac{3}{S}\,\delta_{r,\alpha}(g)
=\frac{3}{(1-r^2)^{\alpha+1}}\,\delta_{r,\alpha}(g).
\]
\end{numlemma}

\begin{proof}
Set $u:=g^2$ and $\delta:=\delta_{r,\alpha}(g)$. Since $\int_{\D}u\,dA_\alpha=1$,
\[
\int_{|z|<r}u\,dA_\alpha=W(1-\delta),
\qquad
\int_{r<|z|<1}u\,dA_\alpha=S+W\delta.
\]

Because $u$ is radial and nondecreasing,
\[
u(\rho)\ge u(r)\quad (\rho\in[r,1)),
\]
hence
\[
u(r)\,S\le \int_{r<|z|<1}u\,dA_\alpha=S+W\delta,
\]
so
\[
u(r)\le 1+\frac{W}{S}\delta.
\]
Likewise, on $[0,r]$ one has $u(\rho)\le u(r)$, hence
\[
\int_{|z|<r}u\,dA_\alpha\le u(r)\,W,
\]
which gives
\[
u(r)\ge 1-\delta.
\]
Thus, with $\kappa:=W/S$,
\[
\sqrt{1-\delta}\le g(r)\le \sqrt{1+\kappa\delta}.
\]

Now split the $L^1(dA_\alpha)$-mass of $g$ into inner and outer parts.

On the outer annulus, monotonicity gives
\[
\int_{r<|z|<1} g\,dA_\alpha \ge S\,g(r)\ge S\sqrt{1-\delta}.
\]

On the inner disk, since $g(\rho)\le g(r)$ for $\rho<r$, we have pointwise
\[
u=g^2\le g(r)\,g,
\]
hence
\[
\int_{|z|<r} g\,dA_\alpha
\ge \frac{\int_{|z|<r}u\,dA_\alpha}{g(r)}
\ge \frac{W(1-\delta)}{\sqrt{1+\kappa\delta}}.
\]

Therefore
\[
\int_{\D} g\,dA_\alpha
\ge
S\sqrt{1-\delta}
+
W\,\frac{1-\delta}{\sqrt{1+\kappa\delta}}
=: \Psi(\delta).
\]
Since $\|g\|_{2,\alpha}=\|1\|_{2,\alpha}=1$,
\[
\|g-1\|_{2,\alpha}^2
=2\Bigl(1-\int_\D g\,dA_\alpha\Bigr)
\le 2(1-\Psi(\delta)).
\]

Now use the elementary bounds
\[
1-\sqrt{1-\delta}\le \frac{\delta}{2},
\qquad
1-\frac{1-\delta}{\sqrt{1+t}}\le \delta+\frac{t}{2}
\qquad (0\le\delta\le 1,\ t\ge0),
\]
with $t=\kappa\delta$. Then
\[
1-\Psi(\delta)
\le
S\frac{\delta}{2}
+
W\Bigl(\delta+\frac{\kappa\delta}{2}\Bigr)
=
\Bigl(\frac S2+W\Bigr)\delta+\frac{W^2}{2S}\delta.
\]
Since $\frac S2+W\le 1$ and $W\le 1$,
\[
1-\Psi(\delta)\le \delta+\frac{1}{2S}\delta\le \frac{3}{2S}\delta.
\]
Hence
\[
\|g-1\|_{2,\alpha}^2\le 2\cdot \frac{3}{2S}\delta=\frac{3}{S}\delta.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{numprop}[Arbitrary sets under monotone bathtub ratio]
\label{prop:bathtub_monotone}
Let $\alpha>-1$ and let $g:\D\to[0,\infty)$ be radial and log-subharmonic.
Assume
\[
\|g\|_{2,\alpha}=1,
\qquad
u(r):=g(r)^2,
\qquad
\Phi_\alpha(r):=u(r)(1-r^2)^{\alpha+2}.
\]
Let $\Omega\subset\D$ be measurable with $\mu(\Omega)=s\in(0,\infty)$, and define
\[
\delta(g;\Omega,\alpha)
:=1-\frac{1}{\theta_\alpha(s)}\int_\Omega g(z)^2\,dA_\alpha(z).
\]
Assume that $\Phi_\alpha$ is nonincreasing on $[0,1)$.
Then
\[
\delta(g;\Omega,\alpha)\ge 0
\quad\text{and}\quad
\|g-1\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le 3\,C_\alpha(s)\,\delta(g;\Omega,\alpha).
\]
\end{numprop}

\begin{proof}
Since $g$ is radial log-subharmonic, it is radial subharmonic, hence nondecreasing in $r$.

Write the angular profile of $\Omega$ as
\[
m(\rho):=\frac1{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\mathbf 1_\Omega(\rho e^{i\theta})\,d\theta\in[0,1].
\]
Then
\[
\int_\Omega u(|z|)\,dA_\alpha(z)
=
2(\alpha+1)\int_0^1 u(\rho)(1-\rho^2)^\alpha \rho\,m(\rho)\,d\rho
=
2(\alpha+1)\int_0^1 \Phi_\alpha(\rho)\,m(\rho)\,d\nu(\rho),
\]
where
\[
d\nu(\rho):=\frac{\rho}{(1-\rho^2)^2}\,d\rho.
\]
Also
\[
\mu(\Omega)=2\pi\int_0^1 m(\rho)\,d\nu(\rho)=s.
\]

Thus maximizing $\int_\Omega u\,dA_\alpha$ among all sets with fixed $\mu$-mass $s$
is exactly a bathtub problem for the weight $\Phi_\alpha$ on $([0,1),d\nu)$.
By the Bathtub principle, an optimizer is a superlevel set of $\Phi_\alpha$.
Since $\Phi_\alpha$ is nonincreasing, its superlevel sets are initial intervals $[0,R)$,
so the maximizer is the centered disk $D_r:=\{|z|<r\}$ with $\mu(D_r)=s$.

Therefore
\[
\int_\Omega u\,dA_\alpha\le \int_{D_r}u\,dA_\alpha.
\]
Because $\theta_\alpha(s)=\int_{D_r}dA_\alpha$, this is equivalent to
\[
\delta(g;\Omega,\alpha)\ge \delta_{r,\alpha}(g)\ge 0.
\]
Applying Lemma~\ref{lem:centered_disk_radial} at radius $r$ yields
\[
\|g-1\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le \frac{3}{(1-r^2)^{\alpha+1}}\delta_{r,\alpha}(g)
\le 3\,C_\alpha(s)\,\delta(g;\Omega,\alpha).
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{numprop}[Disk regime for strictly unimodal bathtub ratio]
\label{thm:disk_regime_unimodal}
Let $\alpha>-1$ and let $g:\D\to[0,\infty)$ be radial and log-subharmonic.
Assume
\[
\|g\|_{2,\alpha}=1,
\qquad
u(r):=g(r)^2,
\qquad
\Phi_\alpha(r):=u(r)(1-r^2)^{\alpha+2}.
\]
Assume that $\Phi_\alpha$ is strictly unimodal: there exists $r_0\in(0,1)$ such that
$\Phi_\alpha$ is strictly increasing on $[0,r_0]$ and strictly decreasing on $[r_0,1)$,
and also $\Phi_\alpha(1^-)=0$.

Let $r_\ast\in(r_0,1)$ be the unique point such that
\[
\Phi_\alpha(r_\ast)=\Phi_\alpha(0),
\]
and set
\[
s_\ast:=\mu(\{|z|<r_\ast\}).
\]
If $\Omega\subset\D$ satisfies $\mu(\Omega)=s\ge s_\ast$, then
\[
\delta(g;\Omega,\alpha)\ge 0
\quad\text{and}\quad
\|g-1\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le 3\,C_\alpha(s)\,\delta(g;\Omega,\alpha).
\]
\end{numprop}

\begin{proof}
As above, maximizing $\int_\Omega u\,dA_\alpha$ under the constraint $\mu(\Omega)=s$
is a bathtub problem for the weight $\Phi_\alpha$ on $([0,1),d\nu)$.
Hence an optimizer is a superlevel set $\{\Phi_\alpha>\lambda\}$.

Because $\Phi_\alpha$ is strictly unimodal, each superlevel set is either

1. a centered disk $[0,R)$ if $\lambda\le \Phi_\alpha(0)$, or

2. an annulus $(R_-(\lambda),R_+(\lambda))$ if $\lambda>\Phi_\alpha(0)$.

Now suppose $\lambda>\Phi_\alpha(0)$. Since $\Phi_\alpha(r_\ast)=\Phi_\alpha(0)$ and
$\Phi_\alpha$ is strictly decreasing on $[r_0,1)$, one has
\[
R_+(\lambda)<r_\ast.
\]
Thus the annulus $\{\Phi_\alpha>\lambda\}$ is contained in $\{|z|<r_\ast\}$, so
\[
\mu(\{\Phi_\alpha>\lambda\})<\mu(\{|z|<r_\ast\})=s_\ast.
\]
Hence no annular optimizer can have $\mu$-mass $s\ge s_\ast$.

Therefore, for every $s\ge s_\ast$, the optimizer must be the centered disk $D_r$
with $\mu(D_r)=s$. Consequently,
\[
\int_\Omega u\,dA_\alpha\le \int_{D_r}u\,dA_\alpha,
\]
which is equivalent to
\[
\delta(g;\Omega,\alpha)\ge \delta_{r,\alpha}(g)\ge 0.
\]
Since radial log-subharmonic functions are nondecreasing, Lemma~\ref{lem:centered_disk_radial} applies and gives
\[
\|g-1\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le \frac{3}{(1-r^2)^{\alpha+1}}\delta_{r,\alpha}(g)
\le 3\,C_\alpha(s)\,\delta(g;\Omega,\alpha).
\]
\end{proof}



\begin{numprop}[A single absolute comparison constant, $\alpha=0$]
\label{prop:absolute_comparison_constant_alpha0}
Let
\[
u(r)=1+\sum_{k=1}^n a_k r^k,\qquad a_k\ge 0,
\]
and define
\[
g(z):=\sqrt{u(|z|)},\qquad \widetilde g:=\frac{g}{\|g\|_{2,0}},
\qquad
dA_0(z):=\frac1\pi\,dA(z).
\]
Let
\[
d\mu(z):=\frac{dA(z)}{(1-|z|^2)^2},
\qquad
\mu(\Omega)=s>0,
\qquad
r=r(s)\ \text{by}\ \mu(\{|z|<r\})=s,
\]
so that
\[
r^2=\frac{s}{s+\pi},
\qquad
\theta_0(s)=\frac{s}{s+\pi}=r^2,
\qquad
C_0(s)=\frac{1}{1-r^2}=1+\frac{s}{\pi}.
\]
Define
\[
\delta(\widetilde g;\Omega,0)
:=1-\frac{1}{\theta_0(s)}\int_\Omega \widetilde g(z)^2\,dA_0(z),
\]
and similarly
\[
\delta(\widetilde g;D_r,0)
:=1-\frac{1}{r^2}\int_{|z|<r}\widetilde g(z)^2\,dA_0(z).
\]
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:absolute_comparison_constant}
\delta(\widetilde g;D_r,0)
\le
K_\ast\,\delta(\widetilde g;\Omega,0),
\qquad
K_\ast:=\frac{25\sqrt5}{25\sqrt5-24}.
\end{equation}
Consequently,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:absolute_stability_constant}
\|\widetilde g-1\|_{2,0}^2
\le
3K_\ast\,C_0(s)\,\delta(\widetilde g;\Omega,0),
\end{equation}
and hence
\[
\|\widetilde g-1\|_{2,0}
\le
C_\ast\,C_0(s)^{1/2}\,\delta(\widetilde g;\Omega,0)^{1/2},
\qquad
C_\ast:=\bigl(3K_\ast\bigr)^{1/2}
=
\Bigl(3\,\frac{25\sqrt5}{25\sqrt5-24}\Bigr)^{1/2}.
\]
\end{numprop}

\begin{proof}
Set
\[
\widetilde u(r):=\widetilde g(r)^2=\frac{u(r)}{\|g\|_{2,0}^2},
\qquad
\varphi(r):=\widetilde u(r)(1-r^2)^2.
\]

Because $a_k\ge 0$, the function $u$ is radial and nondecreasing, hence so is $\widetilde g$.
Therefore Lemma~\ref{lem:centered_disk_radial} with $\alpha=0$ gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:disk_radial_step_abs_const}
\|\widetilde g-1\|_{2,0}^2
\le
3\,C_0(s)\,\delta(\widetilde g;D_r,0).
\end{equation}
So it remains only to compare the disk deficit to the arbitrary-set deficit.

First, since $dA_0=\frac1\pi dA$ and $d\mu=\frac{dA}{(1-|z|^2)^2}$,
\[
\int_\Omega \widetilde u(|z|)\,dA_0(z)
=
\frac1\pi\int_\Omega \varphi(|z|)\,d\mu(z).
\]
Hence, for every measurable $\Omega$ with $\mu(\Omega)=s$,
\[
\int_\Omega \widetilde u\,dA_0
\le
\frac{s}{\pi}\,\max_{0\le t<1}\varphi(t).
\]
Since $\theta_0(s)=\frac{s}{s+\pi}$, this implies
\[
\frac{1}{\theta_0(s)}\int_\Omega \widetilde u\,dA_0
\le
\max\varphi,
\]
and therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:omega_deficit_lower_abs_const}
\delta(\widetilde g;\Omega,0)\ge 1-\max\varphi.
\end{equation}

On the other hand, because $\widetilde u$ is radial and nondecreasing,
\[
\frac{1}{r^2}\int_{|z|<r}\widetilde u\,dA_0 \ge \widetilde u(0),
\]
hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:disk_deficit_upper_abs_const}
\delta(\widetilde g;D_r,0)\le 1-\widetilde u(0).
\end{equation}

We now estimate $\max\varphi$ in terms of $\widetilde u(0)$.
Write
\[
N:=\|g\|_{2,0}^2
=
\int_\D u(|z|)\,dA_0
=
1+\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{2a_k}{k+2}.
\]
Then
\[
\widetilde u(0)=\frac1N,
\qquad
1-\widetilde u(0)=\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{2a_k}{(k+2)N}.
\]
Also,
\[
\widetilde u(r)=\frac1N+\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{a_k}{N}r^k,
\]
so
\[
\varphi(r)
=
\widetilde u(r)(1-r^2)^2
\le
\widetilde u(0)+\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{a_k}{N}r^k(1-r^2)^2.
\]
Rewrite
\[
\frac{a_k}{N}
=
\frac{2a_k}{(k+2)N}\cdot \frac{k+2}{2},
\]
to get
\[
\varphi(r)
\le
\widetilde u(0)
+
\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{2a_k}{(k+2)N}
\Bigl(\frac{k+2}{2}r^k(1-r^2)^2\Bigr).
\]
Hence
\[
\max\varphi
\le
\widetilde u(0)
+
\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{2a_k}{(k+2)N} M_k,
\]
where
\[
M_k:=\max_{0\le r<1}\frac{k+2}{2}\,r^k(1-r^2)^2.
\]

A direct calculation shows that $M_k$ is decreasing in $k\ge 1$, so
\[
M_k\le M_1.
\]
Since
\[
M_1=\max_{0\le r<1}\frac32\,r(1-r^2)^2
=\frac{24}{25\sqrt5},
\]
it follows that
\[
\max\varphi
\le
\widetilde u(0)+\frac{24}{25\sqrt5}\,(1-\widetilde u(0)).
\]
Therefore
\[
1-\max\varphi
\ge
\Bigl(1-\frac{24}{25\sqrt5}\Bigr)(1-\widetilde u(0)).
\]
Combining this with \eqref{eq:omega_deficit_lower_abs_const} and
\eqref{eq:disk_deficit_upper_abs_const}, we obtain
\[
\delta(\widetilde g;D_r,0)
\le
\frac{1-\widetilde u(0)}{1-\max\varphi}\,
\delta(\widetilde g;\Omega,0)
\le
\frac{1}{1-\frac{24}{25\sqrt5}}\,
\delta(\widetilde g;\Omega,0),
\]
which is exactly \eqref{eq:absolute_comparison_constant}.

Finally, insert \eqref{eq:absolute_comparison_constant} into
\eqref{eq:disk_radial_step_abs_const} to get
\[
\|\widetilde g-1\|_{2,0}^2
\le
3K_\ast\,C_0(s)\,\delta(\widetilde g;\Omega,0).
\]
Taking square roots gives the last claim.
\end{proof}



\section[Stability of Faber-Krahn]{Stability of concentration estimates for \texorpdfstring{$\mathcal{M}$}{M}-logsubharmonic functions}

Let $\mathbb{B}=\mathbb{B}^{n}$ be the unit ball in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$.
A mapping $u\in C^{2}(\mathbb{B}^{n}, \mathbb{C})$ or more generally
$u\in C^{2}(\mathbb{B}^{n}, \mathbb{R}^k)$ is said to be \emph{hyperbolic harmonic}
if $u$ (see Rudin~\cite{rudin} and Stoll~\cite{stoll}) satisfies the hyperbolic Laplace equation
\begin{equation}\label{hyphar}
\Delta_{h}u(x)=(1-|x|^2)^2\Delta u(x)
+2(n-2)(1-|x|^2)\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x_{i}}(x)=0,
\end{equation}
where $\Delta$ denotes the usual Laplacian in $\mathbb{R}^{n}$.
We call $\Delta_{h}$ the \emph{hyperbolic Laplacian operator}.
For $x\in \mathbb{B}$ we define the area element of the unit ball by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:invariant-measure-hyperbolic}
d\tau(x) = \frac{1}{(1-|x|^2)^n}\, dV(x).
\end{equation}
We will also sometimes denote hyperbolic measure of the set $A$ by $|A|_h= \tau(A)$.
The Poisson kernel for $\Delta_h$ is defined by
\[
P_h(x,\zeta)=\frac{(1-|x|^2)^{n-1}}{|x-\zeta|^{2n-2}}, \qquad (x,\zeta)\in \mathbb{B}\times \mathbb{S}.
\]
Let $\mathcal{M}$ be the group of conformal automorphisms of the unit ball onto itself.
Then for fixed $\zeta$, $x\mapsto P_h(x,\zeta)$ is $\mathcal{M}$-harmonic, and, given a map
$f\in \mathcal{L}(\mathbb{S})$, the function
\[
u(x) = P_h[f](x):=\int_{\mathbb{S}}P_h(x,\zeta) f(\zeta)\, d\sigma(\zeta)
\]
is the Poisson extension of $f$: it is $\mathcal{M}$-harmonic in $\mathbb{B}$, and agrees with $f$ on $\mathbb{S}$.

We say that a smooth, real function $u$ is $\mathcal{M}$-subharmonic if $\Delta_h u(x) \ge 0$\footnote{This definition can be extended to the case of upper semicontinuous functions, by using the so-called invariant mean value property \cite{stoll}}.
Notice further that
\begin{equation}\label{fol}
(\Delta_h u)(m(x)) =\Delta_h (u\circ m)(x),
\end{equation}
for every M\"obius transformation $m\in\mathcal{M}$ of the unit ball onto itself.
For this reason, we shall also call hyperbolic harmonic (resp.\ hyperbolic subharmonic)
functions $\mathcal{M}$-harmonic (resp.\ $\mathcal{M}$-subharmonic) functions.
Note that, for $n=2$, the $\mathcal{M}$-harmonic and $\mathcal{M}$-subharmonic functions
coincide with the usual Euclidean harmonic and subharmonic functions.

It follows from \eqref{fol}, by putting $u=\mathrm{Id}$, that
\[
\Delta_h m=2 (n-2)(1-|m|^2)m.
\]
Thus, M\"obius transformations are (considered as vectorial functions) hyperbolic harmonic
only in the case $n=2$.

\subsection{Appropriate weights}
In order to construct the weights used in our definition of Bergman spaces, we need to define
a couple of preliminary concepts. Thus, we define the hypergeometric functions $F$ by
\[
F\!\left[\begin{array}{c}
a,b,c\\
u,v
\end{array};t\right]
:=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(a)_{n}(b)_{n}(c)_n}{n!\,(u)_n (v)_n}t^{n},
\qquad |t|<1,
\]
and
\[
F\!\left[\begin{array}{c}
a,b,c
\end{array};t\right]
:=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(a)_{n}(b)_{n}}{n!\,(c)_n }t^{n},
\qquad |t|<1,
\]
and by analytic continuation elsewhere. Here $(a)_n$ denotes the rising factorial,
$(a)_{n}=a(a+1)\cdots(a+n-1)$.

Then one solution to the equation $\Delta_h \log v= -4(n-1)^2$ is given
(see \cite[Proposition~6.1]{kalaj2024}) by
\Needspace{10\baselineskip}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Phi-n}
v=\Phi_n(r)
=\exp\!\left\{\frac{(n-1)(2-n) r^2}{ n}
F\!\left[\begin{array}{c}
1,1,2-\frac{n}{2}\\[2pt]
2,1+\frac{n}{2}
\end{array}; r^2\right]\right\}
(1-r^2)^{n-1}.
\end{equation}
We will sometimes write $\Phi_n(x)$ instead of $\Phi_n(|x|)$.
Note that $\Phi_n(r)\le (1-r^2)^{n-1}$, with strict inequality for $n>2$ and $r>0$.
If $n=2$, then $\Phi_n(|x|)=1-|x|^2$ and this coincides with the case treated in
\cite{RamosTilli}.
Observe, moreover, that $E_n (1-r^2)^{n-1} < \Phi_n(r)$, where
\[
E_n =\exp\!\left\{\frac{(n-1)(2-n)}{ n}
F\!\left[\begin{array}{c}
1,1,2-\frac{n}{2}\\[2pt]
2,1+\frac{n}{2}
\end{array}; 1 \right]\right\}.
\]
This shows that the Bergman norm induced by $\Phi_n$ is equivalent to that induced by
the weight $(1-r^2)^{n-1}.$ The use of these weights appears for the first time in
\cite{kalaj2024}.

\subsection{Admissible monoid}\label{ssec:admissible}
We define $\mathfrak{E}$ to be the set of real-analytic  functions $g$ in $\mathbb{B}$  so that
$f:=\log |g|$ is $\mathcal{M}-$subharmonic. For $n=2$, this class of mappings contains holomorphic functions in the unit disk, however in higher-dimensional case, this is not the case, see \cite{kalaj2024}[Remark 1.2.] for more details on this issue. In other words, for $n=2m\ge 4$, the $\mathfrak{E}$ contains very few holomorphic mappings.

Let  $\mathfrak{E}_+=\{f\in \mathfrak{E}: f>0\}$. Observe that $\mathfrak{E}$ is a monoid where the operation is simply the multiplication of two functions.  Observe that $1=e^0$, so $1\in \mathfrak{E}$. This monoid also contains the Abelian group $\mathcal{G}=\{e^f: \Delta_hf=0\}$. Fos some additional properties of this monoid which is also a convex cone see \cite{kalaj2024} and \cite{kalajramos2024}.



\begin{numdefn}
For $0 < p < \infty$ and $\beta > 1$ we say that a smooth function $f$  in $\mathbb{B}$ belongs to the $\mathcal{M}-$Bergman space ${B}^p_\beta$ if
$$\|f\|_{{B}^p_\beta}^p = c(\beta)\int_\mathbb{B} |f(x)|^p \Phi_n^\beta(|x|) d\tau(x) < \infty,$$ where
$$\frac{1}{c(\beta)}=\left( \int_{\mathbb{B}} \Phi^\beta_n(x)(1-|x|^2)^{-n}\frac{dV(x)}{{\omega_n}}\right).$$
\end{numdefn}
\begin{numdefn}
 For $0 < p < \infty$ and $\beta > 1$ we define
the $\mathcal{M}-$Bergman monoid $\mathbf{B}^p_\beta$ consisting of functions $f$ in ${B}^p_\beta\cap \mathfrak{E}$ having harmonic majorant $F_f\in {B}^p_\beta$.
\end{numdefn}


We first state the general peak-based stability estimate in the hyperbolic
setting. In dimension two this estimate is only preliminary; it will be
upgraded below to the sharp half-stability theorem for positive real-analytic
log-subharmonic functions.

\begin{numthm}[General peak-based hyperbolic stability]\label{stabb}
There exists an explicitly computable constant \(C_{n,\beta}>0\), depending only on
\(n\) and \(\beta\), such that for every measurable set
\(\Omega\subset \mathbb{B}^n\) with \(0<\tau(\Omega)<\infty\) and every
log-\(\mathcal M\)-subharmonic function \(g\in \mathbf{B}^2_\beta\setminus\{0\}\),
one has
\[
\inf_{\substack{|c|=\|g\|_{2,\beta}\\ a\in\mathbb{B}^n}}
\frac{\|g-cg_a\|_{2,\beta}}{\|g\|_{2,\beta}}
\le
C_{n,\beta}\Bigl(C(\tau(\Omega))\,\delta(g;\Omega,\beta)\Bigr)^{\frac1{n+2}},
\]
where
\[
C(\sigma)
=
\left(1-c_\beta\int_0^\sigma v^*(s)\,ds\right)^{-1}
=
\bigl(1-\theta(\sigma)\bigr)^{-1},
\]
and
\[
\theta(\sigma)
=
n c_\beta \int_0^{r(\sigma)}
\rho^{n-1}\Phi_n^\beta(\rho)(1-\rho^2)^{-n}\,d\rho .
\]
\end{numthm}

\begin{numthm}[Preliminary planar quarter-stability]\label{stabb_n2_beta}
Fix $\alpha>-1$ and let $\D=\mathbb{B}^2\subset\mathbb{R}^2$.
Let $\tau$ be the hyperbolic measure on $\D$,
\[
d\tau(x)=\frac{1}{(1-|x|^2)^2}\,dA(x).
\]
There exists an explicitly computable constant $C_{2,\alpha}$ such that for every measurable set
$\Omega\subset\D$ with $0<\tau(\Omega)<\infty$ and every log-subharmonic function
$g\in \mathbf{B}^2_{\alpha}\setminus\{0\}$ we have
\[
\inf_{\substack{|c|=\|g\|_{2,\alpha}\\ a\in\D}}
\frac{\|g-c\,g_a\|_{2,\alpha}}{\|g\|_{2,\alpha}}
\le
C_{2,\alpha}\Bigl(C(\tau(\Omega))\,\delta(g;\Omega)\Bigr)^{\frac14}.
\]

Here the norm is the (normalized) weighted Bergman $L^2$ norm
\[
\|h\|_{2,\alpha}^2 := \frac{\alpha+1}{\pi}\int_{\D} |h(z)|^2(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha}\,dA(z),
\]
and the deficit is
\[
\delta(g;\Omega)
:=
1-\frac{\displaystyle\int_\Omega |g(z)|^2(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha}\,dA(z)}
{\displaystyle\theta_\alpha(\tau(\Omega))\int_{\D}|g(z)|^2(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha}\,dA(z)}\in[0,1].
\]

The extremal family is given by the normalized kernels (up to unimodular constants):
\[
g_a(z)
=
\frac{(1-|a|^2)^{\frac{\alpha+2}{2}}}{|1-\overline{a}z|^{\alpha+2}},
\qquad a\in\D,
\qquad \|g_a\|_{2,\alpha}=1.
\]

Finally, the amplification factor is $C(\sigma)=(1-\theta_\alpha(\sigma))^{-1}$, where
\[
\theta_\alpha(\sigma)=1-\bigl(1-r(\sigma)^2\bigr)^{\alpha+1},
\qquad
\tau(\{|z|<r(\sigma)\})=\sigma.
\]
In dimension $2$ one has explicitly
\[
\tau(\{|z|<r\})=\pi\,\frac{r^2}{1-r^2},
\qquad\text{so}\qquad
r(\sigma)^2=\frac{\sigma}{\sigma+\pi},
\]
and therefore
\[
C(\sigma)=\bigl(1-\theta_\alpha(\sigma)\bigr)^{-1}
=\bigl(1-r(\sigma)^2\bigr)^{-(\alpha+1)}
=\left(\frac{\sigma+\pi}{\pi}\right)^{\alpha+1}.
\]
\end{numthm}

We will start from the sharp pointwise estimate for Bergman spaces of logsubharmonic functions.
The following function has been maximizes in \cite{RamosTilli} for $n=2$ and in \cite{kalajramos2024} for $n>2$.
\begin{equation}\label{newco}
R_n(f,\Omega)=
\frac{c(\beta) \int_\Omega  |f(x)|^2 \Phi_n^\beta(|x|) \, d\tau(x)}{\|f\|^2_{\mathbf{B}^2_\beta}}
\end{equation}
over all $f \in\mathbf{B}^2_\beta$ and $\Omega \subset \mathbb{B}$ with $\tau(\Omega) = s.$

\begin{numthm}\label{thm:main}\cite{RamosTilli, kalajramos2024}
Let $\beta>1,$ and $s>0$ be fixed. Among all functions $f\in \mathbf{B}^2_\beta$ and among
all measurable sets $\Omega\subset  \mathbb{B}$ such that $\tau(\Omega)=s$, the quotient
$R_n(f,\Omega)$ as defined in \eqref{newco} satisfies the inequality
\begin{equation}\label{upper}
	\delta(f,\Omega):=1-\frac{R_n(f,\Omega)}{ R_n(1, \mathbb{B}_s)}\ge 0,
\end{equation}
where $ \mathbb{B}_s$ is a ball centered at the origin with $\tau( \mathbb{B}_s) = s.$
Moreover, there is equality in \eqref{upper} if and only if $f$ belongs to the extremal set
and $\Omega$ is a ball such that $\tau(\Omega)=s$. The extremal set consists of functions
$g(x) = \frac{\Phi_n^{\beta/2}(|m(x)|)}{\Phi_n^{\beta/2}(|x|)}$, where
$m\in\mathcal{M}$ is a M\"obius transformation of the unit ball onto itself.
\end{numthm}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{stabb} for $n=2$]
Write $\D=\mathbb{B}^2$ and
\[
d\tau(z)=\frac{1}{(1-|z|^2)^2}\,dA(z),\qquad \Phi_2(z)=1-|z|^2.
\]
Fix $\alpha>-1$ and let $\Omega\subset\D$ be measurable with $0<\tau(\Omega)=:s_0<\infty$.
By homogeneity we may assume $\|g\|_{2,\alpha}=1$; then
\[
\inf_{\substack{|c|=\|g\|_{2,\alpha}\\ |a|<1}}
\frac{\|g-cg_a\|_{2,\alpha}}{\|g\|_{2,\alpha}}
=\inf_{|a|<1}\|g-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}.
\]

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{1) Rearrangements and the level-set deficit.}
Set
\[
u(z):=(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2}|g(z)|^2,
\qquad
v(z):=(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2}.
\]
Let $u^*,v^*$ be the decreasing rearrangements of $u,v$ with respect to $\tau$.
Define the superlevel set $\Omega_{s_0}:=\{u>u^*(s_0)\}$, so that $\tau(\Omega_{s_0})=s_0$ and
\[
\int_{\Omega}u\,d\tau
\le
\int_{\Omega_{s_0}}u\,d\tau
\stackrel{\tau(\Omega_{s_0})=s_0}{=}
\int_0^{s_0}u^*(s)\,ds
\qquad\text{(Hardy--Littlewood).}
\]
Let
\[
\delta_{s_0}:=
1-\frac{\displaystyle\int_{\Omega_{s_0}}u\,d\tau}{\displaystyle\int_{\mathbb{B}_{s_0}}v\,d\tau}
=
1-\frac{\displaystyle\int_0^{s_0}u^*(s)\,ds}{\displaystyle\int_0^{s_0}v^*(s)\,ds},
\]
where $\mathbb{B}_{s_0}$ denotes the hyperbolic disk centered at the origin with
$\tau(\mathbb{B}_{s_0})=s_0$. Since $\delta(g;\Omega)$ is defined by
\[
\delta(g;\Omega)
=
1-\frac{\displaystyle\int_{\Omega}u\,d\tau}{\displaystyle\int_{\mathbb{B}_{s_0}}v\,d\tau}
\ge
1-\frac{\displaystyle\int_{\Omega_{s_0}}u\,d\tau}{\displaystyle\int_{\mathbb{B}_{s_0}}v\,d\tau}
\stackrel{\mathrm{def}}{=}
\delta_{s_0},
\]
the previous inequality implies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:delta-levelset-fixed}
\delta_{s_0}\le \delta(g;\Omega).
\end{equation}

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{2) Explicit formula for $v^*$ and $\theta_\alpha$.}
For $0<r<1$ one computes
\[
\tau(\{|z|<r\})
=
\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^r \frac{1}{(1-\rho^2)^2}\,\rho\,d\rho\,d\theta
=\pi\,\frac{r^2}{1-r^2}.
\]
Solving
\[
s=\pi\,\frac{r^2}{1-r^2}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
s(1-r^2)=\pi r^2
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
s=r^2(s+\pi),
\]
gives
\[
r^2=\frac{s}{s+\pi},
\qquad
1-r^2=\frac{\pi}{s+\pi}
=\Bigl(1+\frac{s}{\pi}\Bigr)^{-1},
\]
and hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:vstar-n2-fixed}
v^*(s)
=
(1-r^2)^{\alpha+2}
=
\Bigl(1+\tfrac{s}{\pi}\Bigr)^{-(\alpha+2)},
\qquad s>0.
\end{equation}
In particular,
\[
\int_0^{\infty}v^*(s)\,ds=\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}.
\]
Thus
\[
\theta_\alpha(s)
=\frac{\displaystyle\int_0^{s}v^*(t)\,dt}{\displaystyle\int_0^{\infty}v^*(t)\,dt}
=
1-\Bigl(1+\frac{s}{\pi}\Bigr)^{-(\alpha+1)},
\qquad
C(s)=\bigl(1-\theta_\alpha(s)\bigr)^{-1}
=\Bigl(1+\frac{s}{\pi}\Bigr)^{\alpha+1}.
\]

Since $\|g\|_{2,\alpha}=1$, we also have
\[
\int_0^\infty u^*(s)\,ds=\int_0^\infty v^*(s)\,ds=\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}.
\]

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{3) From $\delta_{s_0}$ to a bound on the peak size.}
Let
\[
T:=\|u\|_{L^\infty(\D)}=\sup_{z\in\D}(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2}|g(z)|^2.
\]
By the pointwise estimate
\[
|g(z)|\,\Phi_2(z)^{(\alpha+2)/2}\le \|g\|_{2,\alpha}=1,
\]
we have $0<T\le 1$. Set
\[
s_T:=\pi\bigl(T^{-1/(\alpha+2)}-1\bigr),
\qquad\text{so that}\qquad
v^*(s_T)=T
\]
by \eqref{eq:vstar-n2-fixed}.

We next define the crossing point $s^*$ by
\[
u^*(s^*)=v^*(s^*).
\]
To justify its existence and uniqueness, consider
\[
R(s):=\frac{u^*(s)}{v^*(s)}.
\]
By the rearrangement differential inequality in dimension $2$,
\[
(u^*)'(s)+\frac{\alpha+2}{\pi+s}\,u^*(s)\ge0,
\]
while \eqref{eq:vstar-n2-fixed} gives
\[
(v^*)'(s)+\frac{\alpha+2}{\pi+s}\,v^*(s)=0
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\frac{(v^*)'(s)}{v^*(s)}=-\frac{\alpha+2}{\pi+s}.
\]
Therefore
\[
R'(s)
=
\frac{(u^*)'(s)v^*(s)-(v^*)'(s)u^*(s)}{(v^*(s))^2}
=
\frac{(u^*)'(s)-u^*(s)\frac{(v^*)'(s)}{v^*(s)}}{v^*(s)}
=
\frac{(u^*)'(s)+\frac{\alpha+2}{\pi+s}u^*(s)}{v^*(s)}
\ge0,
\]
so $R$ is nondecreasing.

Now $u^*(0)=T$ and $v^*(0)=1$, hence
\[
R(0)=\frac{u^*(0)}{v^*(0)}=T\le1.
\]
Also,
\[
\int_0^\infty u^*(s)\,ds=\int_0^\infty v^*(s)\,ds.
\]
Thus either $u^*\equiv v^*$ almost everywhere, in which case the conclusion is trivial, or else there exists $s^*\ge0$ such that
\[
R(s^*)=1.
\]
Since $R$ is nondecreasing, we may choose
\[
s^*:=\inf\{s\ge0:\ R(s)=1\},
\]
and then
\[
R(s)\le 1 \ \text{for } 0\le s\le s^*,
\qquad
R(s)\ge 1 \ \text{for } s\ge s^*,
\]
hence
\[
u^*(s)=R(s)v^*(s)\le v^*(s)\quad (0\le s\le s^*),
\qquad
u^*(s)=R(s)v^*(s)\ge v^*(s)\quad (s\ge s^*).
\]

Define
\[
I_*:=\int_0^{s^*}(v^*(s)-u^*(s))\,ds.
\]
We claim that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Istar-upper-n2}
I_* \le \frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\,C(s_0)\,\delta_{s_0}.
\end{equation}
Set
\[
\varepsilon:=\int_0^{s_0}(v^*(s)-u^*(s))\,ds
=\delta_{s_0}\int_0^{s_0}v^*(s)\,ds.
\]

Assume first that $s_0\ge s^*$. Since
\[
\int_0^\infty u^*(s)\,ds=\int_0^\infty v^*(s)\,ds,
\]
we have
\[
\varepsilon
=
\int_0^{s_0}(v^*(s)-u^*(s))\,ds
\stackrel{\int_0^\infty u^*=\int_0^\infty v^*}{=}
\int_{s_0}^{\infty}(u^*(s)-v^*(s))\,ds.
\]
Moreover, for $s\ge s^*$,
\[
u^*(s)-v^*(s)
=
u^*(s)\Bigl(1-\frac{1}{R(s)}\Bigr).
\]
Since $R$ is nondecreasing and $s_0\ge s^*$, it follows that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eps-lower}
\varepsilon
=
\int_{s_0}^{\infty}u^*(s)\Bigl(1-\frac1{R(s)}\Bigr)\,ds
\ge
\Bigl(1-\frac1{R(s_0)}\Bigr)\int_{s_0}^{\infty}u^*(s)\,ds.
\end{equation}
Likewise, for $s\in[s^*,s_0]$ we have
\[
1-\frac1{R(s)}\le 1-\frac1{R(s_0)},
\]
hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:middle-upper}
\int_{s^*}^{s_0}(u^*(s)-v^*(s))\,ds
=
\int_{s^*}^{s_0}u^*(s)\Bigl(1-\frac1{R(s)}\Bigr)\,ds
\le
\Bigl(1-\frac1{R(s_0)}\Bigr)\int_{s^*}^{s_0}u^*(s)\,ds.
\end{equation}

Set
\[
A:=1-\frac1{R(s_0)}\ge0.
\]
If $\int_{s_0}^{\infty}u^*(s)\,ds>0$, then \eqref{eq:eps-lower} implies
\[
A\le
\frac{\varepsilon}{\int_{s_0}^{\infty}u^*(s)\,ds}.
\]
Substituting this into \eqref{eq:middle-upper}, we obtain
\begin{equation}\label{eq:middle-bound}
\int_{s^*}^{s_0}(u^*(s)-v^*(s))\,ds
\le
\varepsilon\,
\frac{\int_{s^*}^{s_0}u^*(s)\,ds}{\int_{s_0}^{\infty}u^*(s)\,ds}.
\end{equation}
Also,
\[
\varepsilon
=
\int_0^{s_0}(v^*(s)-u^*(s))\,ds
=
I_*-\int_{s^*}^{s_0}(u^*(s)-v^*(s))\,ds,
\]
so
\[
I_*=\varepsilon+\int_{s^*}^{s_0}(u^*(s)-v^*(s))\,ds.
\]
Combining this with \eqref{eq:middle-bound} gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Istar-u-ratio}
I_*
=
\varepsilon+\int_{s^*}^{s_0}(u^*(s)-v^*(s))\,ds
\stackrel{\eqref{eq:middle-bound}}{\le}
\varepsilon+\varepsilon\,
\frac{\int_{s^*}^{s_0}u^*(s)\,ds}{\int_{s_0}^{\infty}u^*(s)\,ds}
=
\varepsilon\,
\frac{\int_{s^*}^{\infty}u^*(s)\,ds}{\int_{s_0}^{\infty}u^*(s)\,ds}.
\end{equation}

If instead $\int_{s_0}^{\infty}u^*(s)\,ds=0$, then \eqref{eq:eps-lower} gives $\varepsilon=0$, and
\[
\int_{s^*}^{s_0}(u^*(s)-v^*(s))\,ds=0,
\]
so $I_*=0$ and \eqref{eq:Istar-u-ratio} is trivial.

Finally, since
\[
\int_{s^*}^{\infty}u^*(s)\,ds
\le
\int_0^\infty u^*(s)\,ds
=
\int_0^\infty v^*(s)\,ds
=
\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1},
\]
and since $u^*(s)\ge v^*(s)$ for all $s\ge s_0$,
\[
\int_{s_0}^{\infty}u^*(s)\,ds
\ge
\int_{s_0}^{\infty}v^*(s)\,ds,
\]
we conclude from \eqref{eq:Istar-u-ratio} that
\[
I_*
\le
\varepsilon\,
\frac{\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}}{\int_{s_0}^{\infty}v^*(s)\,ds}.
\]
Since
\[
\varepsilon=\delta_{s_0}\int_0^{s_0}v^*(s)\,ds,
\]
this yields
\[
I_*
\le
\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\,
\frac{\int_0^{s_0}v^*(s)\,ds}{\int_{s_0}^{\infty}v^*(s)\,ds}\,
\delta_{s_0},
\]
which is \eqref{eq:Istar-upper-n2}.
Since
\[
\int_{s_0}^{\infty}v^*(s)\,ds
=
\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\Bigl(1+\frac{s_0}{\pi}\Bigr)^{-(\alpha+1)}
=
\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\,\bigl(1-\theta_\alpha(s_0)\bigr),
\]
it follows that
\[
I_* \le \varepsilon\, C(s_0)\le \frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\,C(s_0)\,\delta_{s_0}.
\]

If $s_0\le s^*$, then
\[
\varepsilon=\int_0^{s_0}v^*(s)\bigl(1-R(s)\bigr)\,ds
\ge
(1-R(s_0))\int_0^{s_0}v^*(s)\,ds,
\]
and
\[
\int_{s_0}^{s^*}(v^*(s)-u^*(s))\,ds
=
\int_{s_0}^{s^*}v^*(s)\bigl(1-R(s)\bigr)\,ds
\le
(1-R(s_0))\int_{s_0}^{s^*}v^*(s)\,ds.
\]
Therefore
\[
I_*=\varepsilon+\int_{s_0}^{s^*}(v^*(s)-u^*(s))\,ds
\le
\varepsilon\,
\frac{\int_0^{s^*}v^*(s)\,ds}{\int_0^{s_0}v^*(s)\,ds}
\le
\delta_{s_0}\int_0^\infty v^*(s)\,ds
=
\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\,\delta_{s_0}
\le
\frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\,C(s_0)\,\delta_{s_0}.
\]
This proves \eqref{eq:Istar-upper-n2}.

Next we compare $I_*$ with the clipped functional. Since $u^*(s)\le T$ for all $s$ and
\[
u^*(s^*)=v^*(s^*)\le T,
\]
the monotonicity of $v^*$ implies
\[
v^*(s^*)\le v^*(s_T)
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
s^*\ge s_T.
\]
Hence
\[
I_*
=
\int_0^{s^*}(v^*(s)-u^*(s))\,ds
\ge
\int_0^{s_T}(v^*(s)-u^*(s))\,ds
\ge
\int_0^{s_T}(v^*(s)-T)\,ds
=:I(T).
\]
A direct computation gives
\begin{align*}
I(T)
&=\int_0^{\pi(T^{-1/(\alpha+2)}-1)}
\Bigl(\bigl(1+\tfrac{s}{\pi}\bigr)^{-(\alpha+2)}-T\Bigr)\,ds\\
&\stackrel{t=1+s/\pi}{=}
\pi\int_1^{T^{-1/(\alpha+2)}}\bigl(t^{-(\alpha+2)}-T\bigr)\,dt\\
&=
\pi\left(
\frac{1-T^{\frac{\alpha+1}{\alpha+2}}}{\alpha+1}
-T\bigl(T^{-1/(\alpha+2)}-1\bigr)
\right)\\
&=
\pi\left(
\frac{1-T^{\frac{\alpha+1}{\alpha+2}}}{\alpha+1}
-T^{\frac{\alpha+1}{\alpha+2}}+T
\right)\\
&=
\pi\left(
T+\frac{1-(\alpha+2)\,T^{\frac{\alpha+1}{\alpha+2}}}{\alpha+1}
\right).
\end{align*}
Moreover,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:I-lower-quad-fixed}
I(T)\ge \frac{\pi}{2(\alpha+2)}\,(1-T)^2,\qquad 0<T\le 1.
\end{equation}

Combining \eqref{eq:Istar-upper-n2} and \eqref{eq:I-lower-quad-fixed}, we obtain
\[
\frac{\pi}{2(\alpha+2)}\,(1-T)^2
\le I(T)\le I_*
\le \frac{\pi}{\alpha+1}\,C(s_0)\,\delta_{s_0},
\]
hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:1minusT-fixed}
(1-T)^2
\le
\frac{2(\alpha+2)}{\alpha+1}\,C(s_0)\,\delta_{s_0}
\le
\frac{2(\alpha+2)}{\alpha+1}\,C(s_0)\,\delta(g;\Omega),
\end{equation}
where in the last step we used \eqref{eq:delta-levelset-fixed}.

\smallskip\noindent
\textbf{4) From the peak size to the distance from extremals.}
Let
\[
g_a(z)=
\frac{(1-|a|^2)^{\frac{\alpha+2}{2}}}{|1-\overline{a}z|^{\alpha+2}},
\qquad a\in\D,
\]
so that $\|g_a\|_{2,\alpha}=1$. Assuming Lemma~\ref{diel} is available for this class,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:diel-n2-fixed}
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
2-2\sup_{|a|<1}\bigl(g(a)\Phi_2(a)^{(\alpha+2)/2}\bigr)
=
2-2\sqrt{T}.
\end{equation}
Using $1-\sqrt{T}\le 1-T$ for $0<T\le1$, we get
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
2-2\sqrt{T}
=
2(1-\sqrt{T}),
\]
so
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}
\le
\sqrt{2(1-\sqrt{T})}
\le
\sqrt{2}\,(1-T)^{1/2}.
\]
Combining this with \eqref{eq:1minusT-fixed} yields
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}
\le
\sqrt{2}\left(\frac{2(\alpha+2)}{\alpha+1}\right)^{1/4}
\bigl(C(s_0)\,\delta(g;\Omega)\bigr)^{1/4}.
\]
Thus Theorem~\ref{stabb} holds for $n=2$ with the explicit constant
\[
C_{2,\alpha}
=
\sqrt{2}\left(\frac{2(\alpha+2)}{\alpha+1}\right)^{1/4}.
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{The planar quarter-stability bottleneck and the sharp half theorem}\label{ssec:sharpness-n2}

The exponent $1/4$ in Theorem~\ref{stabb_n2_beta} arises as the product of two
half-power losses inside the proof:

\smallskip\noindent\emph{(i) Rearrangement-deficit step.} The radial profile of
the level-set deficit yields the \emph{quadratic} bound
\eqref{eq:1minusT-fixed},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sharpness-step-i}
(1-T)^2\;\le\;\frac{2(\alpha+2)}{\alpha+1}\,C(s_0)\,\delta(g;\Omega).
\end{equation}

\smallskip\noindent\emph{(ii) Peak-to-distance step.} The invariant mean-value
inequality (Lemma~\ref{diel}) yields the \emph{linear} bound
\eqref{eq:diel-n2-fixed},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sharpness-step-ii}
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2\;\le\;2-2\sqrt T\;\le\;2(1-T).
\end{equation}

\smallskip
Composing \eqref{eq:sharpness-step-i} and \eqref{eq:sharpness-step-ii} produces
the rate $\delta^{1/4}$. By contrast, in the holomorphic regime of
\cite{GomezKalajMelentijevicRamos2025} both steps are linear (the
quantitative RKHS argument upgrades \eqref{eq:sharpness-step-i} to a linear
estimate), and one obtains the sharp $\delta^{1/2}$.

In this subsection we show that the loss in the log-subharmonic chain is not
genuine. The natural family that saturates
\eqref{eq:sharpness-step-i} is \emph{forced} into a radial regime in which a
direct bathtub argument (Proposition~\ref{prop:bathtub_monotone}) provides a
norm-distance bound $\delta^{1/2}$, bypassing
\eqref{eq:sharpness-step-ii}. Together with the radial and Hilbert-valued
results of the previous section, this identifies Lemma~\ref{diel} as the
source of the artificial quarter exponent. The remainder of the subsection
develops the local and global argument proving that the optimal stability rate
is $\delta^{1/2}$.

\subsubsection*{The natural saturator of \eqref{eq:sharpness-step-i}}

Fix $\alpha>-1$ and $T\in(0,1)$, and let $r_T\in(0,1)$ be the unique solution of
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sharp-rT}
T=(1-r_T^2)^{\alpha+2}.
\end{equation}
Following the radial geometry of $v^*(s)=(1+s/\pi)^{-(\alpha+2)}$, we consider
the radial profile
\begin{equation}\label{eq:saturating-profile}
g_T(z):=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{T^{1/2}}{(1-|z|^2)^{(\alpha+2)/2}},& |z|<r_T,\\[8pt]
\;1,& r_T\le |z|<1.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The function $g_T$ is continuous across the circle $|z|=r_T$ by
\eqref{eq:sharp-rT}, takes the maximal pointwise value $T$ of
$u_{g_T}=g_T^2(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2}$ throughout the disk $\{|z|<r_T\}$, and a
direct computation gives the (un-normalized) Bergman mass
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gT-norm}
\|g_T\|_{2,\alpha}^2
=
(1-r_T^2)^{\alpha+1}\bigl(1+(\alpha+1)r_T^2\bigr).
\end{equation}
Indeed, splitting the integral over $\{|z|<r_T\}$ and $\{r_T\le|z|<1\}$,
\[
\int_{|z|<r_T}\frac{T}{(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2}}\,dA_\alpha(z)
=
T(\alpha+1)\,\frac{r_T^2}{1-r_T^2}
=
(\alpha+1)\,r_T^2(1-r_T^2)^{\alpha+1},
\]
where we used \eqref{eq:sharp-rT}, while
$\int_{r_T<|z|<1}dA_\alpha(z)=(1-r_T^2)^{\alpha+1}$. Adding these gives
\eqref{eq:gT-norm}.

Although $g_T$ has a corner along $|z|=r_T$ that prevents it from being
literally log-subharmonic, the function
$\log g_T(z)=\tfrac12\log T-\tfrac{\alpha+2}{2}\log(1-|z|^2)$ on
$\{|z|<r_T\}$ is smooth subharmonic, and $\log g_T\equiv 0$ on
$\{r_T\le|z|<1\}$. Standard mollification along radii, on a shell of width $\eta$
around $|z|=r_T$, produces a smooth radial log-subharmonic function
$g_T^\eta$ with
\[
\|g_T^\eta-g_T\|_{2,\alpha}\to 0,\qquad
T-\sup_{z\in\D}u_{g_T^\eta}(z)\to 0,\qquad
\delta(g_T^\eta;B_{s_0})\to\delta(g_T;B_{s_0})\qquad (\eta\to0^+),
\]
for every fixed centered hyperbolic ball $B_{s_0}$ of $\tau$-measure
$s_0\ge s_T:=\pi(T^{-1/(\alpha+2)}-1)$. The conclusions below are obtained for
$g_T$ itself; the same statements hold for the smooth approximants $g_T^\eta$ up
to errors that vanish with $\eta$.

\subsubsection*{Saturation of the rearrangement-deficit step}

The pointwise distribution of $u_{g_T}$ is explicit. Indeed, $u_{g_T}\equiv T$
on $\{|z|<r_T\}$, while $u_{g_T}=v$ on $\{|z|\ge r_T\}$, where
$v(z)=(1-|z|^2)^{\alpha+2}$. Consequently, the decreasing rearrangement
$u_{g_T}^*$ with respect to $\tau$ is
\[
u_{g_T}^*(s)
=
\begin{cases}T,& 0\le s\le s_T,\\ v^*(s),& s>s_T.\end{cases}
\]

Pick $s_0\ge s_T$ and consider the centered ball $\Omega=B_{s_0}$. Because
$u_{g_T}$ is radial and $\Phi^{(g_T)}_\alpha(r):=u_{g_T}(z)$ at $|z|=r$ is
nonincreasing (constant equal to $T$ on $[0,r_T]$ and equal to
$(1-r^2)^{\alpha+2}$ on $[r_T,1)$), the bathtub maximizer of
$\int_\Omega u_{g_T}\,d\tau$ at fixed $\tau$-measure $s_0$ is exactly
$B_{s_0}$. Using \eqref{eq:gT-norm} and the same change of variable
$s\mapsto v^*(s)$ as in Step~3 of the proof of
Theorem~\ref{stabb_n2_beta},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gT-deficit}
\delta(g_T;B_{s_0})
=
\frac{1}{\|g_T\|_{2,\alpha}^2\,\theta_\alpha(s_0)}
\int_0^{s_T}\bigl(v^*(s)-T\bigr)\,ds
=
\frac{I(T)}{\|g_T\|_{2,\alpha}^2\,\theta_\alpha(s_0)},
\end{equation}
where $I(T)=\int_0^{s_T}(v^*(s)-T)\,ds$ is precisely the quantity
appearing in \eqref{eq:I-lower-quad-fixed}. Taylor expansion at $T=1$,
already carried out in the proof, yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:I-asymp}
I(T)\;=\;\frac{\pi}{2(\alpha+2)}(1-T)^2+O\bigl((1-T)^3\bigr),\qquad T\to 1^-.
\end{equation}
Combining \eqref{eq:gT-norm}, \eqref{eq:gT-deficit} and \eqref{eq:I-asymp},
and using $\|g_T\|_{2,\alpha}^2\to 1$ as $T\to 1$, we obtain
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gT-deficit-asymp}
\delta(g_T;B_{s_0})
=
\frac{\alpha+1}{2(\alpha+2)\,\theta_\alpha(s_0)}\,(1-T)^2
+O\bigl((1-T)^3\bigr),\qquad T\to 1^-.
\end{equation}
Comparing with \eqref{eq:sharpness-step-i}, we see that $g_T$ saturates the
rearrangement-deficit step up to the universal factor
$\theta_\alpha(s_0)(1-\theta_\alpha(s_0))\in(0,1/4]$:
\[
(1-T)^2
\;=\;
\frac{2(\alpha+2)\,\theta_\alpha(s_0)}{\alpha+1}\,
\delta(g_T;B_{s_0})\,\bigl(1+o(1)\bigr).
\]
In other words, the inequality \eqref{eq:sharpness-step-i} cannot be
quantitatively improved (in its $(1-T)^2$ exponent) within the radial
log-subharmonic class.

\subsubsection*{The chain $\delta^{1/4}$ is loose on the saturator}

If \eqref{eq:sharpness-step-ii} were also saturated on the family $\{g_T\}$,
the chain would force $\inf_a\|g_T-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}\asymp\delta(g_T;B_{s_0})^{1/4}$. We show that this is \emph{not} the case: on $g_T$, the quantity
$\inf_a\|g_T-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2$ is in fact controlled by $(1-T)^2$, not by
$1-T$. Two observations make this precise.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{(a) Application of Proposition~\ref{prop:bathtub_monotone}.}
Normalize by setting $\widetilde g_T:=g_T/\|g_T\|_{2,\alpha}$, so that
$\|\widetilde g_T\|_{2,\alpha}=1$. The function $\widetilde g_T$ remains radial,
nondecreasing in $|z|$, and its bathtub ratio
\[
\Phi_\alpha^{\widetilde g_T}(r)
=
\widetilde g_T(r)^2(1-r^2)^{\alpha+2}
=
\frac{1}{\|g_T\|_{2,\alpha}^2}
\begin{cases}T,& 0\le r\le r_T,\\[2pt](1-r^2)^{\alpha+2},& r_T\le r<1\end{cases}
\]
is constant on $[0,r_T]$ and strictly decreasing on $[r_T,1)$, hence
\emph{nonincreasing} on $[0,1)$. Proposition~\ref{prop:bathtub_monotone} therefore
applies and yields, for every measurable $\Omega\subset\D$ with
$\mu(\Omega)=s_0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gT-bathtub}
\|\widetilde g_T-1\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\;\le\;
3\,C_\alpha(s_0)\,\delta(\widetilde g_T;\Omega,\alpha).
\end{equation}
Since $1=g_0$ in the centered normalization $a=0$, \eqref{eq:gT-bathtub} is
exactly the planar stability inequality with the \emph{sharp} exponent
$1/2$ on this family, and with constants of the same order as in
Theorem~\ref{stabb_n2_beta}. By Möbius covariance, the analogous bound holds with
$1$ replaced by any extremal $g_a$ and $g_T$ replaced by its conjugate by the
Möbius transformation sending $0$ to $a$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{(b) Direct asymptotics of the norm distance.}
The same conclusion may be checked by a direct calculation. Writing
$\widetilde g_T=g_T/\|g_T\|_{2,\alpha}$ and using
$\|\widetilde g_T\|_{2,\alpha}=\|1\|_{2,\alpha}=1$,
\[
\|\widetilde g_T-1\|_{2,\alpha}^2
=
2-2\langle\widetilde g_T,1\rangle_{2,\alpha}
=
2\Bigl(1-\frac{1}{\|g_T\|_{2,\alpha}}\int_\D g_T\,dA_\alpha\Bigr).
\]
For the first integral, splitting again at $|z|=r_T$ and using
\eqref{eq:sharp-rT},
\[
\int_\D g_T\,dA_\alpha
=
T^{1/2}\int_{|z|<r_T}(1-|z|^2)^{-(\alpha+2)/2}dA_\alpha
+\int_{r_T<|z|<1}dA_\alpha,
\]
both integrals being elementary. Using the expansion
$1-r_T^2=T^{1/(\alpha+2)}=1-(1-T)/(\alpha+2)+O((1-T)^2)$, a routine
calculation gives, as $T\to 1^-$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gT-distance-asymp}
\|\widetilde g_T-1\|_{2,\alpha}^2
=
\frac{1}{\alpha+1}\,(1-T)^2
+O\bigl((1-T)^3\bigr).
\end{equation}
In particular, $\|\widetilde g_T-1\|_{2,\alpha}\asymp 1-T$, in stark contrast
with \eqref{eq:sharpness-step-ii} which would predict
$\|\widetilde g_T-1\|_{2,\alpha}\lesssim(1-T)^{1/2}$.

Combining \eqref{eq:gT-deficit-asymp} and \eqref{eq:gT-distance-asymp},
\[
\|\widetilde g_T-1\|_{2,\alpha}
\;\sim\;
\sqrt{\frac{2(\alpha+2)\,\theta_\alpha(s_0)}{\alpha+1}}\,
\delta(\widetilde g_T;B_{s_0})^{1/2}
\qquad (T\to 1^-).
\]
On the natural saturator of \eqref{eq:sharpness-step-i}, the actual norm
distance behaves as $\delta^{1/2}$, not $\delta^{1/4}$. The looseness of
the chain in the proof of Theorem~\ref{stabb_n2_beta} is therefore localized
entirely in step~(ii), Lemma~\ref{diel}.

\subsubsection*{The mechanism: Lemma~\ref{diel} is loose on radial saturators}

The reason \eqref{eq:sharpness-step-ii} is loose on $\widetilde g_T$ can be
isolated explicitly. The proof of Lemma~\ref{diel} bounds
$\langle g,g_a\rangle_{2,\alpha}$ from below by the pointwise value $\sqrt T$
of $g\,\Phi_2^{(\alpha+2)/2}$ at $a$, via the invariant mean-value property of
the log-$\mathcal M$-subharmonic function
\[
h(y)=g(m_a(y))\,\Phi_2^{-(\alpha+2)/2}(y)\,\Phi_2^{(\alpha+2)/2}(m_a(y))
\]
at $y=0$. This is equivalent to using the \emph{first} term in the radial
mean-value expansion of $h$. On $\widetilde g_T$ centered at $a=0$, however,
$h$ coincides with $\widetilde g_T$ and the next mean-value coefficients
exhibit a quadratic improvement, exactly accounting for the gain
$1-\sqrt T\approx (1-T)/2$ to $(1-T)^2$ visible in \eqref{eq:gT-distance-asymp}.

\subsubsection*{The sharp theorem and the peak-only obstruction}

The preceding radial saturator shows that the exponent $1/4$ in
Theorem~\ref{stabb_n2_beta} is not sharp. The rest of this subsection proves
the global replacement, Theorem~\ref{thm:global-half-from-green-absorption},
with exponent $1/2$ for positive real-analytic log-subharmonic functions.
Before entering the local and global proof, we record a complementary warning:
the sharp theorem cannot be obtained by merely improving Lemma~\ref{diel} to a
quadratic estimate in the peak defect $1-T$. Such a peak-only bound is false
even inside the zero-free holomorphic subclass. The proof of the sharp theorem
therefore has to use the full logarithmic structure, through the Riesz
decomposition and the perturbative bathtub analysis developed below.

\begin{numprop}[Failure of a quadratic peak-to-distance estimate]\label{prop:quadratic-diel-fails}
Fix $\alpha>-1$ and set $p:=\alpha+2$. There is no constant $M_\alpha<\infty$
such that every positive log-subharmonic $g$ with $\|g\|_{2,\alpha}=1$ satisfies
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
M_\alpha(1-T)^2,
\qquad
T:=\sup_{z\in\D}g(z)^2(1-|z|^2)^p.
\]
More precisely, for the zero-free holomorphic perturbations
\[
g_\varepsilon(z):=\frac{\exp(\varepsilon\Re z^2)}
{\|\exp(\varepsilon\Re z^2)\|_{2,\alpha}},
\qquad |\varepsilon|\ll1,
\]
one has
\[
1-T_\varepsilon
=
\frac{2}{(\alpha+2)(\alpha+3)}\,\varepsilon^2+O(\varepsilon^4),
\]
while
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g_\varepsilon-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2
=
\frac{1}{(\alpha+2)(\alpha+3)}\,\varepsilon^2+O(|\varepsilon|^3).
\]
\end{numprop}

\begin{proof}
Let $\varphi(z):=\Re z^2$ and let $dA_\alpha$ be the normalized weighted area
measure. Since $\varphi$ has zero angular mean,
\[
\int_\D \varphi\,dA_\alpha=0,
\qquad
\int_\D \varphi^2\,dA_\alpha
=\frac12\int_\D |z|^4\,dA_\alpha
=\frac{1}{(\alpha+2)(\alpha+3)}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\|\exp(\varepsilon\varphi)\|_{2,\alpha}^2
=
\int_\D e^{2\varepsilon\varphi}\,dA_\alpha
=
1+
\frac{2}{(\alpha+2)(\alpha+3)}\,\varepsilon^2
+O(\varepsilon^4).
\]
For $|\varepsilon|<p/2$ the function
\[
2\varepsilon\Re z^2+p\log(1-|z|^2)
\]
is maximized at $z=0$, hence
\[
T_\varepsilon
=\|\exp(\varepsilon\varphi)\|_{2,\alpha}^{-2}
=
1-\frac{2}{(\alpha+2)(\alpha+3)}\,\varepsilon^2+O(\varepsilon^4).
\]

It remains to estimate the distance from the extremal manifold. In the centered
normalization $g_0\equiv1$, and for small $a$,
\[
g_a(z)=1+p\Re(\overline a z)+O(|a|^2)
\quad\text{in }L^2(dA_\alpha).
\]
On the other hand,
\[
g_\varepsilon(z)=1+\varepsilon\Re z^2+O(\varepsilon^2)
\quad\text{in }L^2(dA_\alpha).
\]
The functions $\Re z^2$ and $\Im z^2$ are orthogonal, with respect to the radial
measure $dA_\alpha$, to the tangent space
\[
\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb R}\{\Re z,\Im z\}
\]
of the extremal manifold at $g_0$. The nearest point on the smooth manifold
$\{g_a:|a|<1\}$ therefore has $a=O(\varepsilon^2)$, and the squared distance is
\[
\varepsilon^2\|\Re z^2\|_{2,\alpha}^2+O(|\varepsilon|^3)
=
\frac{1}{(\alpha+2)(\alpha+3)}\,\varepsilon^2+O(|\varepsilon|^3).
\]
This is incompatible with a bound by $M_\alpha(1-T_\varepsilon)^2$, since the
right-hand side would be $O(\varepsilon^4)$.
\end{proof}

The same perturbation also gives the usual sharpness obstruction for any
possible exponent larger than $1/2$. If $B_s$ is the centered hyperbolic disk
of fixed measure $s\in(0,\infty)$, then
\[
\delta(g_\varepsilon;B_s)
=
2\left(
\int_\D\varphi^2\,dA_\alpha
-
\frac{1}{\theta_\alpha(s)}\int_{B_s}\varphi^2\,dA_\alpha
\right)\varepsilon^2
+O(\varepsilon^4),
\]
and the coefficient is strictly positive. Hence
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g_\varepsilon-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}
\asymp
\delta(g_\varepsilon;B_s)^{1/2},
\qquad \varepsilon\to0.
\]
Thus the exponent $1/2$ in
Theorem~\ref{thm:global-half-from-green-absorption} is optimal.

\subsubsection*{A second-variation route}

The failure of Proposition~\ref{prop:quadratic-diel-fails} suggests that the
right replacement for Lemma~\ref{diel} is not another estimate depending only
on the peak $T$. A more robust approach is to work directly near the extremal
manifold. In centered coordinates the extremal is $g_0\equiv1$, and a positive
log-subharmonic perturbation can be written as
\[
g_\varepsilon(z)=
\frac{\exp(\varepsilon\varphi(z))}
{\|\exp(\varepsilon\varphi)\|_{2,\alpha}},
\qquad \Delta\varphi\ge0.
\]
The linearized cone already contains the main rigidity: for subharmonic
$\varphi$,
\[
\frac1{\theta_\alpha(s)}\int_{B_s}\varphi\,dA_\alpha
\le
\int_\D\varphi\,dA_\alpha,
\]
with equality for harmonic $\varphi$. Thus genuinely subharmonic directions
create a first-order deficit, while harmonic directions must be controlled by
the second variation. The latter part is completely diagonal.

\begin{numprop}[Second variation in harmonic directions]\label{prop:harmonic-second-variation}
Fix $\alpha>-1$ and a centered hyperbolic disk $B_s=\{|z|<r\}$, where
$0<r<1$, and write $\theta=\theta_\alpha(s)=dA_\alpha(B_s)$.
Let $h$ be a real-valued harmonic polynomial with
\[
\int_\D h\,dA_\alpha=0,
\qquad
h(z)=\sum_{k\ge1} h_k(z),
\qquad
h_k(z)=\Re(b_k z^k).
\]
Set
\[
g_\varepsilon:=
\frac{e^{\varepsilon h}}{\|e^{\varepsilon h}\|_{2,\alpha}}.
\]
Then, as $\varepsilon\to0$,
\[
\delta(g_\varepsilon;B_s)
=
2\varepsilon^2
\sum_{k\ge1}\lambda_k(s)\,\|h_k\|_{2,\alpha}^2
+O(|\varepsilon|^3),
\]
where
\[
\lambda_k(s)
:=
1-
\frac{\displaystyle\int_{B_s}|z|^{2k}\,dA_\alpha}
{\displaystyle\theta\int_\D |z|^{2k}\,dA_\alpha}
\in(0,1).
\]
Equivalently, with $R=r^2$,
\[
\lambda_k(s)
=
1-
\frac{\displaystyle\int_0^R t^k(1-t)^\alpha\,dt}
{\displaystyle\bigl(1-(1-R)^{\alpha+1}\bigr)
\int_0^1 t^k(1-t)^\alpha\,dt}.
\]
Moreover,
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g_\varepsilon-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2
=
\varepsilon^2\sum_{k\ge2}\|h_k\|_{2,\alpha}^2
+O(|\varepsilon|^3).
\]
Consequently, with
\[
\Lambda_{\alpha,s}:=\inf_{k\ge2}\lambda_k(s)>0,
\]
one has the local estimate
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g_\varepsilon-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
\frac{1}{2\Lambda_{\alpha,s}}\,
\delta(g_\varepsilon;B_s)
+O(|\varepsilon|^3).
\]
\end{numprop}

\begin{proof}
Since $dA_\alpha$ is radial, distinct harmonic modes are orthogonal in
$L^2(dA_\alpha)$, and the same is true after restriction to the centered disk
$B_s$. Also $\int_\D h\,dA_\alpha=\int_{B_s}h\,dA_\alpha=0$. Expanding
\[
\|e^{\varepsilon h}\|_{2,\alpha}^2
=
\int_\D e^{2\varepsilon h}\,dA_\alpha
=
1+2\varepsilon^2\int_\D h^2\,dA_\alpha+O(|\varepsilon|^3)
\]
and similarly on $B_s$, we obtain
\[
\int_{B_s}g_\varepsilon^2\,dA_\alpha
=
\theta
+2\varepsilon^2
\left(\int_{B_s}h^2\,dA_\alpha
-\theta\int_\D h^2\,dA_\alpha\right)
+O(|\varepsilon|^3).
\]
The stated expansion of the deficit follows by dividing by $\theta$ and using
orthogonality of the modes. The positivity of $\lambda_k(s)$ is just the strict
inequality between the conditional $2k$-moment of $|z|$ on the disk $B_s$ and
the corresponding moment on the full disk; the infimum over $k\ge2$ is positive
because $\lambda_k(s)\to1$ as $k\to\infty$.

For the distance expansion, note that
\[
g_\varepsilon=1+\varepsilon h+O(\varepsilon^2)
\quad\text{in }L^2(dA_\alpha),
\]
while the extremal manifold has tangent space at $g_0\equiv1$ given by
\[
\{p\Re(\overline a z):a\in\C\},\qquad p=\alpha+2.
\]
Indeed,
\[
g_a(z)=
\frac{(1-|a|^2)^{p/2}}{|1-\overline a z|^p}
=
1+p\Re(\overline a z)+O(|a|^2)
\quad\text{in }L^2(dA_\alpha).
\]
Optimizing over $a$ therefore removes precisely the $k=1$ harmonic component
to first order, and the claimed squared-distance expansion follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{numcor}[Local half-stability in the log-harmonic sector]\label{cor:local-harmonic-half}
Fix $\alpha>-1$ and $s\in(0,\infty)$. There exist constants
$\eta_{\alpha,s}>0$ and $C_{\alpha,s}<\infty$ with the following property.
Let $h$ be a real-valued harmonic function in a neighbourhood of $\overline\D$,
with $\int_\D h\,dA_\alpha=0$ and $\|h\|_{L^\infty(\D)}\le\eta_{\alpha,s}$, and set
\[
g_h:=\frac{e^h}{\|e^h\|_{2,\alpha}}.
\]
Then
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g_h-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
C_{\alpha,s}\,\delta(g_h;B_s).
\]
Equivalently, in this neighbourhood of the extremal manifold,
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g_h-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}
\le
C_{\alpha,s}^{1/2}\,\delta(g_h;B_s)^{1/2}.
\]
\end{numcor}

\begin{proof}
Write the harmonic expansion
\[
h=h_1+h_{\ge2},
\qquad
h_1(z)=\Re(b_1z),\qquad
h_{\ge2}=\sum_{k\ge2}h_k.
\]
By Proposition~\ref{prop:harmonic-second-variation}, applied to the path
$\varepsilon h$ at $\varepsilon=1$, and by choosing $\|h\|_{L^\infty}$ small
enough to absorb the cubic remainder, we have
\begin{equation}\label{eq:local-harmonic-deficit-lower}
\delta(g_h;B_s)
\ge
\sum_{k\ge1}\lambda_k(s)\|h_k\|_{2,\alpha}^2.
\end{equation}
Here $\lambda_k(s)>0$ for every $k$, and
\[
\Lambda_{\alpha,s}:=\inf_{k\ge2}\lambda_k(s)>0.
\]

The map $a\mapsto g_a$ is a smooth two-dimensional submanifold of
$L^2(dA_\alpha)$ near $a=0$, with tangent space
\[
\mcalT_0=\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb R}\{\Re z,\Im z\}.
\]
The nearest-point projection onto this submanifold is $C^2$ in a small
neighbourhood of $g_0=1$. Since
\[
g_h
=1+h+O(\|h\|_{L^\infty}^2)
\quad\text{in }L^2(dA_\alpha),
\]
and since the component $h_1$ lies exactly in $\mcalT_0$, the standard
tubular-neighbourhood estimate gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:local-harmonic-distance-upper}
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g_h-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
2\|h_{\ge2}\|_{2,\alpha}^2
+C_\alpha\|h\|_{L^\infty}^4.
\end{equation}
After decreasing $\eta_{\alpha,s}$ if necessary, the fourth-order term is
absorbed by the right-hand side of
\eqref{eq:local-harmonic-deficit-lower}; indeed, if $h_{\ge2}=0$, then
$g_h$ is already tangent to the extremal manifold to first order and the
left-hand side is $O(\|h_1\|^4)$, while
\eqref{eq:local-harmonic-deficit-lower} is $\gtrsim\|h_1\|^2$ unless
$h_1=0$. Thus
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g_h-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
\frac{4}{\Lambda_{\alpha,s}}\,
\delta(g_h;B_s),
\]
after changing constants. This proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{numrem}[Why this is the correct local model]
The preceding corollary applies to zero-free holomorphic functions after taking
$h=\log|f|-\int_\D\log|f|\,dA_\alpha$. It therefore contains both the tangent
directions $k=1$, which correspond to moving along the extremal manifold, and
the first genuinely transverse holomorphic perturbations $k\ge2$, such as
$\Re z^2$. The latter are exactly the perturbations in
Proposition~\ref{prop:quadratic-diel-fails}, where the exponent $1/2$ is seen
to be sharp.
\end{numrem}

\subsubsection*{Local coercivity on the subharmonic cone}

We now close the local log-subharmonic argument. Let
\[
P(F):=\int_\D F\,dA_\alpha,\qquad
Q_s(F):=\frac1{\theta_\alpha(s)}\int_{B_s}F\,dA_\alpha,
\]
and normalize real perturbations by $P(\varphi)=0$. For
$g_\varphi=e^\varphi/\|e^\varphi\|_{2,\alpha}$, a direct Taylor expansion gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:deficit-second-expansion-general}
\delta(g_\varphi;B_s)
=
-2Q_s(\varphi)
+2\bigl(P(\varphi^2)-Q_s(\varphi^2)\bigr)
+O(\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}\|\varphi\|_{2,\alpha}^2).
\end{equation}
If $\varphi$ is subharmonic, then $Q_s(\varphi)\le P(\varphi)=0$, because the
circular mean of a subharmonic function is increasing in the radius and
$B_s$ is a centered disk. Thus the first term in
\eqref{eq:deficit-second-expansion-general} is nonnegative and controls the
genuinely subharmonic directions to first order. The only directions in which
this first variation vanishes are harmonic directions, and those are controlled
by Corollary~\ref{cor:local-harmonic-half}.

\begin{numlemma}[Green-potential coercivity]\label{lem:green-potential-coercivity}
Fix $\alpha>-1$ and $s\in(0,\infty)$. Let $G(z,\zeta)$ be the Green function of
the disk, normalized so that $G(\cdot,\zeta)\ge0$ and $G(\cdot,\zeta)=0$ on
$\partial\D$. If
\[
\psi(z)=-\int_\D G(z,\zeta)\,d\mu(\zeta),
\qquad \mu\ge0,
\]
is bounded in $\D$, then
\[
\|\psi-P(\psi)\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
C_{\alpha,s}\,\|\psi\|_{L^\infty(\D)}\,
\bigl(P(\psi)-Q_s(\psi)\bigr).
\]
\end{numlemma}

\begin{proof}
Set
\[
A_s(\psi):=P(\psi)-Q_s(\psi).
\]
For the Green kernel define
\[
H_{\alpha,s}(\zeta):=
P_z[-G(z,\zeta)]-Q_{s,z}[-G(z,\zeta)].
\]
Since $z\mapsto -G(z,\zeta)$ is subharmonic and has zero boundary values,
the weighted mean over the smaller centered disk is no larger than the weighted
mean over the whole disk; hence $H_{\alpha,s}(\zeta)\ge0$. In fact
$H_{\alpha,s}(\zeta)>0$ for $\zeta\in\D$. Moreover, by the usual boundary
asymptotics of the Green function,
\[
H_{\alpha,s}(\zeta)\asymp_{\alpha,s} 1-|\zeta|
\qquad (|\zeta|\to1^-),
\]
and
\[
\|-G(\cdot,\zeta)-P_z[-G(z,\zeta)]\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\lesssim_{\alpha,s}
(1-|\zeta|)\,\|-G(\cdot,\zeta)\|_{L^\infty(\{|z-\zeta|> (1-|\zeta|)/2\})}.
\]
For bounded Green potentials this kernel estimate integrates against
$d\mu(\zeta)$ after the standard truncation
$G_N=\min\{G,N\}$ and monotone convergence. Equivalently, applying the estimate
to $\psi_N=-\int G_N\,d\mu$ and letting $N\to\infty$ gives
\[
\|\psi-P(\psi)\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
C_{\alpha,s}\|\psi\|_{L^\infty}
\int_\D H_{\alpha,s}(\zeta)\,d\mu(\zeta).
\]
By linearity of $P-Q_s$,
\[
\int_\D H_{\alpha,s}(\zeta)\,d\mu(\zeta)
=
P(\psi)-Q_s(\psi),
\]
which proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{numprop}[Local cone coercivity]\label{prop:local-cone-coercivity}
For every $\alpha>-1$ and $s\in(0,\infty)$ there exist
$\eta_{\alpha,s}>0$ and $K_{\alpha,s}<\infty$ such that every real-analytic
subharmonic $\varphi$ on a neighbourhood of $\overline\D$ with
$P(\varphi)=0$ and $\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}\le\eta_{\alpha,s}$ satisfies
\[
\inf_{\ell\in\mcalT_0}\|\varphi-\ell\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
K_{\alpha,s}\left[
-Q_s(\varphi)+P(\varphi^2)-Q_s(\varphi^2)
\right],
\]
where $\mcalT_0=\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb R}\{\Re z,\Im z\}$.
\end{numprop}

\begin{proof}
Use the Riesz decomposition in the disk:
\[
\varphi=h+\psi,\qquad
\psi(z)=-\int_\D G(z,\zeta)\,\frac{\Delta\varphi(\zeta)}{2\pi}\,dA(\zeta),
\]
where $h$ is harmonic in a neighbourhood of $\overline\D$ and $\psi\le0$.
Since $P$ and $Q_s$ are radial averages, they agree on harmonic functions:
\[
P(h)=Q_s(h)=h(0).
\]
The normalization $P(\varphi)=0$ gives $P(h)=-P(\psi)$, and therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:first-variation-potential}
-Q_s(\varphi)=P(\psi)-Q_s(\psi)\ge0.
\end{equation}

Write $h=h_1+h_{\ge2}+h(0)$, where
$h_1\in\mcalT_0$ and $h_{\ge2}$ is the sum of harmonic modes of order at least
two. The harmonic spectral-gap computation in
Proposition~\ref{prop:harmonic-second-variation} gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:harmonic-gap-local-cone}
\|h_{\ge2}\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
C_{\alpha,s}\bigl(P(h^2)-Q_s(h^2)\bigr).
\end{equation}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:green-potential-coercivity} and
$\|\psi\|_\infty\le 2\|\varphi\|_\infty$ after decreasing the neighbourhood if
necessary,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:potential-gap-local-cone}
\|\psi-P(\psi)\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
C_{\alpha,s}\eta_{\alpha,s}\,\bigl(P(\psi)-Q_s(\psi)\bigr).
\end{equation}

Now expand the quadratic part:
\[
P(\varphi^2)-Q_s(\varphi^2)
=
P(h^2)-Q_s(h^2)
+2\bigl(P(h\psi)-Q_s(h\psi)\bigr)
+P(\psi^2)-Q_s(\psi^2).
\]
The two terms containing $\psi$ are controlled by Cauchy's inequality, using
\eqref{eq:potential-gap-local-cone}. More precisely, for any fixed small
$\varepsilon>0$,
\[
\big|P(h\psi)-Q_s(h\psi)\big|+\big|P(\psi^2)-Q_s(\psi^2)\big|
\le
\varepsilon\|h_{\ge2}\|_{2,\alpha}^2
C_{\alpha,s,\varepsilon}\eta_{\alpha,s}\bigl(P(\psi)-Q_s(\psi)\bigr).
\]
Choosing first $\varepsilon$ and then $\eta_{\alpha,s}$ small, and using
\eqref{eq:first-variation-potential} and \eqref{eq:harmonic-gap-local-cone},
we obtain
\[
\|h_{\ge2}\|_{2,\alpha}^2+\|\psi-P(\psi)\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
K_{\alpha,s}\left[
-Q_s(\varphi)+P(\varphi^2)-Q_s(\varphi^2)
\right].
\]
Finally,
\[
\inf_{\ell\in\mcalT_0}\|\varphi-\ell\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
2\|h_{\ge2}\|_{2,\alpha}^2+2\|\psi-P(\psi)\|_{2,\alpha}^2,
\]
because the constants cancel through $P(\varphi)=0$ and $h_1$ is the tangent
component. This proves the proposition.
\end{proof}

\begin{numthm}[Local sharp half-stability for log-subharmonic perturbations]\label{thm:local-half-logsubharmonic}
For every $\alpha>-1$ and $s\in(0,\infty)$ there are constants
$\eta_{\alpha,s}>0$ and $C_{\alpha,s}<\infty$ such that, whenever
$\varphi$ is real-analytic and subharmonic on a neighbourhood of
$\overline\D$, $P(\varphi)=0$, and
$\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}\le\eta_{\alpha,s}$, the function
$g_\varphi=e^\varphi/\|e^\varphi\|_{2,\alpha}$ satisfies
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g_\varphi-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
C_{\alpha,s}\,\delta(g_\varphi;B_s).
\]
\end{numthm}

\begin{proof}
The Taylor expansion of the normalized exponential map gives
\[
g_\varphi
=1+\varphi+O(\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}^2)
\quad\text{in }L^2(dA_\alpha).
\]
As in the proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:local-harmonic-half}, the extremal
manifold has tangent space $\mcalT_0$ at $g_0=1$, hence the tubular estimate
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g_\varphi-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
2\inf_{\ell\in\mcalT_0}\|\varphi-\ell\|_{2,\alpha}^2
+C_\alpha\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}\|\varphi\|_{2,\alpha}^2.
\]
By Proposition~\ref{prop:local-cone-coercivity}, the first term is bounded by
the quadratic expression on the right-hand side of
\eqref{eq:deficit-second-expansion-general}. Choosing
$\eta_{\alpha,s}$ small enough, the error term is absorbed into the same
quantity, and \eqref{eq:deficit-second-expansion-general} yields
\[
\inf_{|a|<1}\|g_\varphi-g_a\|_{2,\alpha}^2
\le
C_{\alpha,s}\,\delta(g_\varphi;B_s).
\]
\end{proof}

This theorem is the local form of the sharp $1/2$ stability theorem. We now turn an
arbitrary near-extremizer into this normal form, after applying a Mobius
transformation and discarding the tangent directions, through the route
\[
\text{near equality}
\Longrightarrow
\text{compactness to some }g_a
\Longrightarrow
\text{second-variation estimate at }g_a.
\]
The real-analytic log-subharmonic assumption enters in the compactness step
through the Riesz decomposition: the harmonic part is handled by
Proposition~\ref{prop:harmonic-second-variation}, while the negative
Green-potential part contributes to the first variation with the correct sign.
This avoids the peak-only bottleneck responsible for the exponent $1/4$.

The obstacle one has to remove is that the logarithmic chart is not compact in
$L^\infty$.
