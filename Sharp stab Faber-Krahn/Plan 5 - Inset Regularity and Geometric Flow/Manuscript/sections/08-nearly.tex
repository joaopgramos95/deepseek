\section{Perturbative stability of nearly spherical sets}
\label{sec:nearly}

This section proves, in full and self-containedly, the perturbative
torsional stability of small-amplitude radial graphs consumed by the
reduction of \cref{sec:main}. The result is a Fuglede-type estimate
\emph{for the torsion functional}: a planar set whose boundary is a graph of
small amplitude over a circle, after the natural volume normalisation, has
scale-invariant torsional deficit controlling the square of its Fraenkel
asymmetry. We invoke no selection principle, no spectral black box, and no
quantitative isoperimetric inequality.

The graph is taken about an \emph{arbitrary} centre $z$: we do not assume
that $z$ is the barycentre, nor do we recentre the set. The only structural
information beyond the volume constraint is that the centre be chosen so that
the first Fourier mode of the profile --- the translation mode --- is
\emph{quadratically} small, $\|P_1\varphi\|_{L^2}\le K\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$.
This is an explicit, checkable condition that the constant and first modes
play symmetric, neutral roles, and it is the natural minimal hypothesis: it
holds for the barycentre exactly, and we verify in \cref{sec:main} that it
holds for the $L^1$-optimal centre produced by the construction. Recentring
the set to enforce $P_1\varphi=0$ is \emph{not} used, and would be unsafe
under the present hypotheses, because the profile is only Lipschitz with
slope that may degenerate.

The point requiring care is that the graph produced by \cref{sec:fold} has
\emph{small amplitude but no slope control}: its radial profile is only
\emph{measurable}, with no modulus of continuity and no bounded variation
available. The
classical nearly-spherical estimates (Fuglede~\cite{Fuglede1989} and their
torsional analogues) assume the boundary is a $C^1$- or $W^{2,\infty}$-small
graph; they do not apply here, and it is precisely to manufacture such
regularity that a selection principle is otherwise introduced. We therefore
give a proof valid under an $L^\infty$ amplitude bound alone. The mechanism
is an exact energy identity together with a \emph{slope-free} annular
Steklov lower bound that is diagonal in the boundary data; the coercive
output is at the $L^2$ (Fraenkel) scale, which is all that is needed and all
that survives at high frequency.

\subsection{Statement and standing normalisation}

For a measurable $\varphi\colon S^1\to\R$ with $\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}<1$, a
radius $\hat r_G>0$ and a centre $z\in\R^2$ we write
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Gdef}
   G=G(z,\hat r_G,\varphi):=\set{z+r\omega(\theta):\ \theta\in S^1,\
   0\le r<\hat r_G\,(1+\varphi(\theta))},\qquad \omega(\theta)=(\cos\theta,\sin\theta),
\end{equation}
the radial graph of profile $\varphi$. We recall that for a bounded open
$E\subset\R^2$ of finite measure with torsion function $u_E\in H^1_0(E)$
($-\Delta u_E=1$) and $T(E)=\int_E u_E$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dstarAdef}
   \dstar(E)=\frac{T(B_E)-T(E)}{T(B_E)},\quad T(B_E)=\frac{|E|^2}{8\pi},
   \qquad
   \AF(E)=\inf_{x_0}\frac{|E\triangle B(x_0,r_E)|}{|E|},\ r_E=\sqrt{|E|/\pi},
\end{equation}
are the scale-invariant torsional deficit and the Fraenkel asymmetry; both
are invariant under translations and dilations.

\begin{theorem}[Radial-graph torsion stability]\label{thm:graphG}
Fix any $K<\infty$. There are constants $\eta_G\in(0,\tfrac1{13}]$ and
$C_G<\infty$, depending only on $K$ \textup{(}one may take
\begin{equation}\label{eq:graphG-const}
   \eta_G=\min\Bigl\{\tfrac1{80},\ \tfrac1{16(1+K)^2}\Bigr\},
   \qquad C_G=3
\end{equation}
\textup{)} such that the following holds. Let $G=G(z,\hat r_G,\varphi)$ be a
radial graph as in \eqref{eq:Gdef} about an \emph{arbitrary} centre
$z\in\R^2$, with $\varphi\colon S^1\to\R$ \emph{measurable} \textup{(}so that
$G$ is a bounded open star-shaped set about $z$, taken in its open
representative\textup{)},
$\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}\le\eta_G$, and
\[
   \hat r_G=\sqrt{|G|/\pi}.
\]
Assume the \emph{quadratic mode-one bound}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mode1hyp}
   \|P_1\varphi\|_{L^2(S^1)}\ \le\ K\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2(S^1)}^2,
\end{equation}
where $P_1$ is the projection onto $\{\cos\theta,\sin\theta\}$ in the polar
parametrisation of $\partial G$ about $z$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:graphG}
   \AF(G)^2\ \le\ C_G\,\dstar(G).
\end{equation}
The statement is scale-invariant; $G$ need not have area $\pi$. It uses no
property of the original set $\Om$ and no selection. No slope, perimeter,
curvature, barycentre or recentring hypothesis is made: only the volume
constraint $\hat r_G=\sqrt{|G|/\pi}$, the $L^\infty$ amplitude bound, and the
quadratic mode-one bound \eqref{eq:mode1hyp}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:noslope}
The hypotheses are on the $L^\infty$ \emph{amplitude} of $\varphi$, the
volume constraint, and the quadratic mode-one bound \eqref{eq:mode1hyp}: no
slope, perimeter, curvature, $C^1$ or $C^2$ bound is assumed. The amplitude
bound serves only to trap $\partial G$ in a thin annulus; the volume
constraint removes the constant mode quantitatively; and \eqref{eq:mode1hyp}
removes the first (translation) mode quantitatively, playing exactly the role
that a barycentre normalisation would. This is exactly the regime delivered
by \cref{prop:realization}, where the amplitude is $O(\dT^{1/8})\to0$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[The mode-one hypothesis and the barycentre]\label{rem:mode1}
The quadratic mode-one bound \eqref{eq:mode1hyp} holds, with $K$ absolute,
whenever $z$ is the barycentre of $G$: then $\int_{S^1}R^3\,\omega\d\theta=0$,
and expanding $R^3=1+3\varphi+3\varphi^2+\varphi^3$ together with
$\int_{S^1}\omega=0$ gives
$3\int\varphi\,\omega=-\int(3\varphi^2+\varphi^3)\omega$, so
$\|P_1\varphi\|_{L^2}\le\tfrac1{\sqrt\pi}(1+\tfrac13\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty})
\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$. Thus \cref{thm:graphG} contains the
barycentre-normalised case as the special instance
$K=\tfrac1{\sqrt\pi}(1+\tfrac1{39})$ \textup{(}using
$\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}\le\tfrac1{13}$\textup{)}. The point of stating it as the
explicit condition \eqref{eq:mode1hyp} is that it can also be verified
\emph{at a prescribed centre} --- in particular at the $L^1$-optimal centre
of the construction (\cref{sec:main}) --- without recentring the set, which
under an $L^\infty$ amplitude bound alone need not preserve single-valuedness
of the radial graph.
\end{remark}

Since $\dstar$ and $\AF$ are dilation- and translation-invariant, replacing
$G$ by $\hat r_G^{-1}(G-z)$ we assume throughout the proof
\begin{equation}\label{eq:norm}
   z=0,\qquad \hat r_G=1,\qquad |G|=\pi,
\end{equation}
where the last equality uses the hypothesis
$\hat r_G=\sqrt{|G|/\pi}$. (This rescaling fixes the origin at the prescribed
centre $z$ and leaves \eqref{eq:mode1hyp} unchanged, since both sides scale
the same way under $\varphi\mapsto\hat r_G^{-1}\varphi$ once $\hat r_G$ is
normalised to $1$.) Thus $R:=1+\varphi$ satisfies the single volume
constraint
\begin{equation}\label{eq:constraints}
   \int_{S^1}\bigl(R^2-1\bigr)\d\theta=0
   \quad\Bigl(\text{i.e. }\int_{S^1}(2\varphi+\varphi^2)\d\theta=0\Bigr),
\end{equation}
which is just $|G|=\tfrac12\int R^2=\pi$. We do \emph{not} impose any
barycentre constraint; the first Fourier mode of $\varphi$ is controlled
instead through the quadratic mode-one hypothesis \eqref{eq:mode1hyp}. We
write $u_0(x):=\tfrac14(1-|x|^2)$
for the disc torsion, $-\Delta u_0=1$, $u_0|_{\partial B_1}=0$,
$T(B_1)=\pi/8$. Expand $\varphi$ and the boundary datum
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hdef}
   h(\theta):=\frac{R(\theta)^2-1}{4}=\frac{2\varphi+\varphi^2}{4}
   =\frac\varphi2+\frac{\varphi^2}4
\end{equation}
in real Fourier series, with $P_k$ the projection onto the $k$-th mode
$\{\cos k\theta,\sin k\theta\}$ (for $k=0$ the constants) and
$P_{\ge2}=\sum_{k\ge2}P_k$. Recall $\|\psi\|_{L^2(S^1)}^2=\sum_{k\ge0}
\|P_k\psi\|_{L^2}^2$.

\subsection{An exact energy identity}

Write the energy functional and the (negative) torsion energy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Jdef}
   J_E(v):=\tfrac12\int_E|\nabla v|^2-\int_E v\ \ (v\in H^1_0(E)),
   \qquad
   \mathcal E(E):=\min_{v\in H^1_0(E)}J_E(v)=J_E(u_E)=-\tfrac12 T(E),
\end{equation}
the last equality because $\int_E|\nabla u_E|^2=\int_E u_E=T(E)$. Thus
$\mathcal E(G)-\mathcal E(B_1)=\tfrac12\bigl(T(B_1)-T(G)\bigr)$, and on the
normalisation \eqref{eq:norm},
$\dstar(G)=\tfrac8\pi\bigl(\tfrac\pi8-T(G)\bigr)
=\tfrac{16}\pi\bigl(\mathcal E(G)-\mathcal E(B_1)\bigr)$.

\begin{lemma}[Exact graph identity]\label{lem:identity}
Let $G$ be any bounded \emph{open} star-shaped \textup{(}about $0$\textup{)}
set with $B_{\rho_i}\subset G\subset B_{\rho_e}$ and \emph{measurable} radial
profile $R=\rho\colon S^1\to[\rho_i,\rho_e]$, normalised by \eqref{eq:norm}.
Let $z:=u_G-u_0\in H^1(G)$. Then $z$ is harmonic in $G$, with $z=h$ as its
a.e.-radial boundary trace \textup{(}$h$ from \eqref{eq:hdef}\textup{)}, and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:identity8}
   \mathcal E(G)-\mathcal E(B_1)
   =\frac12\int_G|\nabla z|^2\d x
     -\frac1{32}\int_{S^1}\bigl(R^4-1\bigr)\d\theta .
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Both $u_G$ and $u_0$ solve $-\Delta(\cdot)=1$, so $z=u_G-u_0$ is harmonic in
$G$. The boundary trace is read off ray by ray: for a.e.\ $\theta$ the
restriction of $u_G$ to the radial segment $r\mapsto u_G(r\omega(\theta))$
lies in $H^1(0,\rho(\theta))$, is absolutely continuous, and (being the
restriction of an $H^1_0(G)$ function) has radial limit $0$ at the endpoint
$r=\rho(\theta)$; hence $z=u_G-u_0$ has radial limit $-u_0(\rho\omega)
=\tfrac14(\rho^2-1)=h(\theta)$ there. We emphasise, however, that the
identity \eqref{eq:identity8} below uses \emph{no} boundary regularity: it is
proved by a boundary-term-free Green identity valid for any bounded open $G$.

\emph{A weak Green identity.}
Since $u_0\in C^\infty(\R^2)$ with $-\Delta u_0=1$ and $u_G\in H^1_0(G)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weakgreen}
   \int_G\nabla u_0\cdot\nabla u_G\,dx=\int_G u_G\,dx=T(G).
\end{equation}
Indeed, choose $\phi_j\in C_c^\infty(G)$ with $\phi_j\to u_G$ in $H^1_0(G)$;
integrating by parts on the compact support of $\phi_j$ (no boundary term),
$\int_G\nabla u_0\cdot\nabla\phi_j=\int_G(-\Delta u_0)\phi_j=\int_G\phi_j$,
and passing to the limit in $H^1_0(G)$ (the left side is continuous in
$\nabla\phi_j$ because $\nabla u_0\in L^\infty(G)$, the right side in
$\phi_j$) yields \eqref{eq:weakgreen}. No boundary regularity of $G$ is
invoked.

\emph{Polar integrals for the smooth datum.}
By Fubini in polar coordinates, valid for the measurable profile $\rho$,
\begin{align}
   \int_G|\nabla u_0|^2\,dx
      &=\int_{S^1}\!\int_0^{\rho}\frac{r^2}4\,r\,dr\d\theta
      =\frac1{16}\int_{S^1}\rho^4\d\theta,
      \label{eq:polar-grad}\\
   \int_G u_0\,dx
      &=\int_{S^1}\!\int_0^{\rho}\frac{1-r^2}4\,r\,dr\d\theta
      =\int_{S^1}\Bigl(\frac{\rho^2}8-\frac{\rho^4}{16}\Bigr)\d\theta,
      \label{eq:polar-u0}
\end{align}
using $|\nabla u_0|^2=r^2/4$ and $u_0=\tfrac14(1-r^2)$.

\emph{Expansion of $T(G)$.}
Write $\nabla u_G=\nabla u_0+\nabla z$. Then
\[
   T(G)=\int_G|\nabla u_G|^2
   =\int_G|\nabla u_0|^2+2\int_G\nabla u_0\cdot\nabla z+\int_G|\nabla z|^2 .
\]
By \eqref{eq:weakgreen} and \eqref{eq:polar-grad},
\[
   \int_G\nabla u_0\cdot\nabla z
   =\int_G\nabla u_0\cdot\nabla u_G-\int_G|\nabla u_0|^2
   =T(G)-\frac1{16}\int_{S^1}\rho^4\d\theta .
\]
Substituting,
\[
   T(G)=\frac1{16}\int\rho^4
   +2\Bigl(T(G)-\frac1{16}\int\rho^4\Bigr)+\int_G|\nabla z|^2,
\]
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Tgz}
   T(G)=\frac1{16}\int_{S^1}\rho^4\d\theta-\int_G|\nabla z|^2\d x .
\end{equation}
(On the disc $\rho\equiv1$ this reads $T(B_1)=\tfrac1{16}\cdot2\pi=\pi/8$,
as it must.)

\emph{Conversion to $\mathcal E$.}
Recall $\mathcal E(E)=-\tfrac12 T(E)$ and, on the normalisation
\eqref{eq:norm}, $\mathcal E(B_1)=-\tfrac\pi{16}$. From \eqref{eq:Tgz},
\[
   \mathcal E(G)-\mathcal E(B_1)
   =-\frac12 T(G)+\frac\pi{16}
   =-\frac1{32}\int_{S^1}\rho^4\d\theta+\frac12\int_G|\nabla z|^2
     +\frac\pi{16},
\]
and since $\tfrac\pi{16}=\tfrac1{32}\int_{S^1}1\d\theta$, the two
$\rho^4$/constant terms combine into $-\tfrac1{32}\int_{S^1}(\rho^4-1)$.
With $R=\rho$ this is exactly \eqref{eq:identity8}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{A slope-free annular Steklov lower bound}

The next lemma is the heart of the matter: it bounds the Dirichlet energy of
\emph{any} $H^1$ function with prescribed outer trace $h$ from below by a
diagonal, $L^2$-coercive functional of $h$ --- with no reference to the
slope of $\partial G$.

Assume $G$ is a bounded open star-shaped set with measurable profile
$1-\eta\le R\le1+\eta$, $0<\eta\le\tfrac14$, and set $\rho:=1-\eta$ (so that
$B_\rho\subset G$). Expand $h=\sum_{k\ge0}h_k$ into Fourier modes.

\begin{lemma}[Annular Steklov lower bound]\label{lem:annular}
Let $w\in H^1(G)$ have, for a.e.\ $\theta$, a radial trace
$w(R(\theta)\theta)=h(\theta)$ at the outer endpoint of the ray. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:annular}
   \int_G|\nabla w|^2\d x\ \ge\ b(\eta)\sum_{k\ge2}\|h_k\|_{L^2(S^1)}^2,
   \qquad
   b(\eta):=\frac{2\mu(\eta)}{2+\mu(\eta)},\quad \mu(\eta):=\frac{1-\eta}{2\eta},
\end{equation}
and $b(\eta)\uparrow2$ as $\eta\downarrow0$; in particular $b(\eta)\ge\tfrac32$
whenever $\eta\le\tfrac1{13}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $f(\theta):=w(\rho\,\theta)$ be the trace of $w$ on the inner circle
$\partial B_\rho\subset G$ (it lies in $G$ since $\rho=1-\eta\le R$). Split
$\int_G|\nabla w|^2=\int_{B_\rho}|\nabla w|^2+\int_{G\setminus B_\rho}
|\nabla w|^2$.

\emph{Inner ball.} Among all $H^1(B_\rho)$ functions with trace $f$ on
$\partial B_\rho$ the Dirichlet energy is minimised by the harmonic
extension, so with $f=\sum_k f_k$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inner}
   \int_{B_\rho}|\nabla w|^2\ \ge\ \sum_{k\ge1}k\,\|f_k\|_{L^2(S^1)}^2
\end{equation}
(in the plane the harmonic extension of the mode $f_k$ is $(r/\rho)^k$ times
it, with Dirichlet energy $k\|f_k\|_{L^2}^2$, independent of $\rho$).

\emph{Annular shell.} By Fubini, for a.e.\ $\theta$ the restriction
$r\mapsto w(r\omega(\theta))$ lies in $H^1(\rho,R(\theta))$, is absolutely
continuous on $[\rho,R(\theta)]$, equals $f(\theta)$ at $r=\rho$, and has
radial limit $h(\theta)$ at the outer endpoint $r=R(\theta)$ by hypothesis;
no regularity of the profile $R$ is used. Along the radial segment
$r\in[\rho,R(\theta)]$ Cauchy--Schwarz then gives
\[
   |h(\theta)-f(\theta)|^2
   =\Bigl(\int_\rho^{R(\theta)}\!\partial_r w\,dr\Bigr)^2
   \le\Bigl(\int_\rho^{R(\theta)}\!|\partial_r w|^2\,r\,dr\Bigr)
      \Bigl(\int_\rho^{R(\theta)}\!\frac{dr}{r}\Bigr).
\]
Since $R\le1+\eta$, $\rho=1-\eta$ and $\log\tfrac{1+t}{1-t}\le\tfrac{2t}{1-t}$
for $t\in[0,\tfrac14]$,
$\int_\rho^{R}\tfrac{dr}r=\log\tfrac{R}\rho\le\log\tfrac{1+\eta}{1-\eta}\le
\tfrac{2\eta}{1-\eta}=\mu(\eta)^{-1}$. Hence
$\int_\rho^{R(\theta)}|\partial_r w|^2 r\,dr\ge\mu(\eta)|h(\theta)-f(\theta)|^2$,
and integrating in $\theta$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell}
   \int_{G\setminus B_\rho}|\nabla w|^2\ \ge\ \mu(\eta)\,\|h-f\|_{L^2(S^1)}^2
   =\mu(\eta)\sum_{k\ge0}\|h_k-f_k\|_{L^2}^2 .
\end{equation}

\emph{Mode-by-mode minimisation.} Adding \eqref{eq:inner} and
\eqref{eq:shell} (and dropping the $k=0$ shell term) gives the lower bound
$\sum_{k\ge1}\bigl(k\|f_k\|^2+\mu\|h_k-f_k\|^2\bigr)$. Minimising
$k|f_k|^2+\mu|h_k-f_k|^2$ over $f_k$ yields $\frac{k\mu}{k+\mu}|h_k|^2$. The
coefficient $\frac{k\mu}{k+\mu}$ is increasing in $k$, so for $k\ge2$ it is
at least its value at $k=2$, namely $\frac{2\mu}{2+\mu}=b(\eta)$. This is
\eqref{eq:annular}. Finally $b(\eta)\ge\tfrac32\iff\mu\ge6\iff
\frac{1-\eta}{2\eta}\ge6\iff\eta\le\tfrac1{13}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Removing the neutral modes}

\begin{lemma}[Neutral modes]\label{lem:modes}
Assume $\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}\le\tfrac1{13}$, the volume constraint
\eqref{eq:constraints}, and the mode-one hypothesis \eqref{eq:mode1hyp} with
parameter $K$. Then, with $C_0:=\tfrac1{2\sqrt{2\pi}}+K$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:neutral}
   \|P_0\varphi\|_{L^2}+\|P_1\varphi\|_{L^2}\ \le\ C_0\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2 .
\end{equation}
Consequently, if $\|\varphi\|_{L^2}\le\eps_1:=1/(\sqrt{32}\,C_0)$, then
$\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2}^2\ge\tfrac{15}{16}\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The volume constraint $\int_{S^1}(2\varphi+\varphi^2)=0$ gives
$2\int\varphi=-\int\varphi^2$, so the mean obeys
$|\overline\varphi|=\tfrac1{2\pi}|\int\varphi|=\tfrac1{4\pi}\int\varphi^2$ and
$\|P_0\varphi\|_{L^2}=\sqrt{2\pi}\,|\overline\varphi|
=\tfrac1{2\sqrt{2\pi}}\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$. The first-mode term is bounded
directly by the hypothesis \eqref{eq:mode1hyp}, $\|P_1\varphi\|_{L^2}\le
K\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$. Adding the two bounds gives \eqref{eq:neutral} with
$C_0=\tfrac1{2\sqrt{2\pi}}+K$. Then
$\|P_0\varphi\|^2+\|P_1\varphi\|^2\le2C_0^2\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^4\le
\tfrac1{16}\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$ once $\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2\le\eps_1^2=
1/(32C_0^2)$, and the last claim follows from
$\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|^2=\|\varphi\|^2-\|P_0\varphi\|^2-\|P_1\varphi\|^2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Boundary datum and moment]\label{lem:taylor}
If $\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}\le\eta\le\tfrac1{13}$ and \eqref{eq:constraints}
holds, then
\begin{align}
   \int_{S^1}\bigl(R^4-1\bigr)\d\theta
      &\le\ \bigl(4+4\eta+\eta^2\bigr)\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2 ,
      \label{eq:moment}\\
   \|P_{\ge2}h\|_{L^2}^2
      &\ge\ \tfrac14\,\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2}^2
            -\tfrac14\,\eta\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2 .
      \label{eq:hlow}
\end{align}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Expand $R^4-1=4\varphi+6\varphi^2+4\varphi^3+\varphi^4$. By
\eqref{eq:constraints}, $4\int\varphi=-2\int\varphi^2$, so
\[
   \int_{S^1}(R^4-1)=4\int\varphi^2+\int(4\varphi^3+\varphi^4)
   \le\bigl(4+4\eta+\eta^2\bigr)\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2,
\]
using $\int|4\varphi^3+\varphi^4|\le(4\eta+\eta^2)\int\varphi^2$; this is
\eqref{eq:moment}. For \eqref{eq:hlow}, from \eqref{eq:hdef},
$P_{\ge2}h=\tfrac12P_{\ge2}\varphi+\tfrac14P_{\ge2}(\varphi^2)$ with
$\|P_{\ge2}(\varphi^2)\|_{L^2}\le\|\varphi^2\|_{L^2}\le\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}
\|\varphi\|_{L^2}\le\eta\|\varphi\|_{L^2}$. Hence, by the triangle inequality,
$\|P_{\ge2}h\|_{L^2}\ge\tfrac12\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2}-\tfrac14\eta
\|\varphi\|_{L^2}$, and both terms are nonnegative; squaring and discarding
the nonnegative square term,
\[
   \|P_{\ge2}h\|_{L^2}^2\ge\tfrac14\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2}^2
   -2\cdot\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac14\eta\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2}\|\varphi\|_{L^2}
   \ge\tfrac14\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2}^2-\tfrac14\eta\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2,
\]
using $\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2}\le\|\varphi\|_{L^2}$ in the last step.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of \texorpdfstring{\cref{thm:graphG}}{the theorem}}

Fix $K$ and set $\eta_G:=\min\{\tfrac1{80},\,\tfrac1{16(1+K)^2}\}$ as in
\eqref{eq:graphG-const}. We prove \eqref{eq:graphG} for every $\varphi$
satisfying the hypotheses of the theorem, i.e.\ the volume constraint
\eqref{eq:constraints}, the mode-one bound \eqref{eq:mode1hyp}, and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:thresh}
   \|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}\le\eta_G\le\tfrac1{80}.
\end{equation}
We first check that \cref{lem:modes,lem:taylor} apply with
$\eta=\eta_G\le\tfrac1{13}$. From \eqref{eq:thresh},
$\|\varphi\|_{L^2}\le\sqrt{2\pi}\,\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}\le\sqrt{2\pi}\,\eta_G$.
With $C_0=\tfrac1{2\sqrt{2\pi}}+K<1+K$ and
$\eps_1=1/(\sqrt{32}\,C_0)$ from \cref{lem:modes}, the smallness
$\|\varphi\|_{L^2}\le\eps_1$ needed there holds because
\[
   \sqrt{2\pi}\,\eta_G\,\cdot\,\sqrt{32}\,C_0
   \ <\ \sqrt{64\pi}\,(1+K)\,\eta_G
   \ \le\ \sqrt{64\pi}\,(1+K)\,\frac1{16(1+K)^2}
   \ =\ \frac{\sqrt{64\pi}}{16(1+K)}\ <\ 1,
\]
using $\sqrt{64\pi}<16$ and $1+K\ge1$. Thus
$\|\varphi\|_{L^2}<\eps_1$ and \cref{lem:modes} gives
$\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|^2\ge\tfrac{15}{16}\|\varphi\|^2$, while \cref{lem:taylor}
applies with $\eta=\eta_G$. In the application,
\cref{prop:realization} delivers $\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}\le C\dT^{1/8}\to0$,
so \eqref{eq:thresh} holds for $\dT$ small, and \cref{sec:main} supplies a
centre for which \eqref{eq:mode1hyp} holds.

By \cref{lem:annular} applied to $w=z$ (legitimate: $z$ is harmonic with
outer trace $h$, and $1-\eta_G\le R\le1+\eta_G$ with $\eta_G\le\tfrac1{13}$,
so $b(\eta_G)\ge\tfrac32$), then \eqref{eq:hlow} and the
$\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|^2\ge\tfrac{15}{16}\|\varphi\|^2$ of \cref{lem:modes},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:zlow}
   \int_G|\nabla z|^2
   \ \ge\ \tfrac32\,\|P_{\ge2}h\|_{L^2}^2
   \ \ge\ \tfrac32\Bigl(\tfrac14\cdot\tfrac{15}{16}-\tfrac14\eta_G\Bigr)
          \|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2
   \ =\ \Bigl(\tfrac{45}{128}-\tfrac38\eta_G\Bigr)\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2 .
\end{equation}
Combining \eqref{eq:zlow} and the moment bound \eqref{eq:moment} in the exact
identity \eqref{eq:identity8},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:deffinal}
\begin{aligned}
   \mathcal E(G)-\mathcal E(B_1)
   &\ge\ \frac12\Bigl(\tfrac{45}{128}-\tfrac38\eta_G\Bigr)\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2
        -\frac1{32}\bigl(4+4\eta_G+\eta_G^2\bigr)\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2\\
   &=\ \Bigl(\tfrac{45}{256}-\tfrac18-\tfrac5{16}\eta_G-\tfrac1{32}\eta_G^2\Bigr)
        \|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2
   \ =\ \Bigl(\tfrac{13}{256}-\tfrac5{16}\eta_G-\tfrac1{32}\eta_G^2\Bigr)
        \|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2 .
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The bracket is decreasing in $\eta_G$, so its minimum over $\eta_G\le\tfrac1{80}$
is attained at $\eta_G=\tfrac1{80}$, where it equals
$\tfrac{13}{256}-\tfrac5{1280}-\tfrac1{204800}=0.04687\ge0.046=:c_0$; hence
for every admissible $\eta_G\le\tfrac1{80}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Edown}
   \mathcal E(G)-\mathcal E(B_1)\ \ge\ c_0\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2,
   \qquad c_0=0.046,
\end{equation}
and therefore, by $\dstar(G)=\tfrac{16}\pi(\mathcal E(G)-\mathcal E(B_1))$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dstarlow}
   \dstar(G)\ \ge\ \frac{16 c_0}\pi\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2
   \ =\ \frac{0.736}\pi\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2
   \ \ge\ 0.234\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2 .
\end{equation}

Finally we bound the asymmetry. Comparing $G$ with $B_1$,
\[
   |G\triangle B_1|=\int_{S^1}\Bigl|\int_0^{R}r\,dr-\int_0^1 r\,dr\Bigr|\d\theta
   =\frac12\int_{S^1}|R^2-1|\d\theta
   =\frac12\int_{S^1}|2\varphi+\varphi^2|\d\theta
   \le\bigl(1+\tfrac{\eta_G}2\bigr)\!\int_{S^1}\!|\varphi|\d\theta,
\]
so by Cauchy--Schwarz $|G\triangle B_1|\le(1+\tfrac{\eta_G}2)\sqrt{2\pi}\,
\|\varphi\|_{L^2}$, and since $\AF(G)\le|G\triangle B_1|/\pi$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Aupper}
   \AF(G)^2\ \le\ \frac{2(1+\eta_G/2)^2}\pi\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2
   \ \le\ \frac{2(1+1/160)^2}\pi\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2\ \le\ 0.645\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2 ,
\end{equation}
where in the last two steps we used $\eta_G\le\tfrac1{80}$. Combining
\eqref{eq:Aupper} with \eqref{eq:dstarlow},
\[
   \AF(G)^2\ \le\ 0.645\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2
   \ \le\ \frac{0.645}{0.234}\,\dstar(G)\ =\ 2.76\,\dstar(G)\ \le\ 3\,\dstar(G),
\]
which is \eqref{eq:graphG} with $C_G=3$.\qed

\begin{remark}[Sharpness and what is used]\label{rem:sharp}
The exponent $2$ in \eqref{eq:graphG} is sharp: for $\varphi=a\cos2\theta$
(a small ellipse) one has $\AF(G)^2\sim c_1a^2$ and $\dstar(G)\sim c_2a^2$ as
$a\to0$ with $c_1,c_2>0$, so $\AF(G)^2/\dstar(G)$ tends to a finite positive
limit and no exponent larger than $2$ can hold. The proof uses only: the
exact energy identity \cref{lem:identity} (integration by parts and the
radial-graph area element); the Dirichlet principle and a one-dimensional
Cauchy--Schwarz along radii (\cref{lem:annular}); the harmonicity of the
disc modes $r^k e^{\mathrm i k\theta}$; Parseval; the volume constraint; and
the quadratic mode-one bound \eqref{eq:mode1hyp}. There is \emph{no}
quantitative isoperimetric inequality, \emph{no} selection or symmetrisation,
\emph{no} second shape derivative, \emph{no} barycentre recentring of the
set, and crucially \emph{no} slope or curvature control on $\partial G$ ---
only the $L^\infty$ amplitude bound. The coercive output is at the $L^2$
(Fraenkel) scale; the stronger $\dot H^{1/2}$ bound is false at this level of
generality (high-frequency ripples) but is not needed. The constants
\eqref{eq:graphG-const} are not optimised; the threshold $\eta\le1/13$ for
$b(\eta)\ge3/2$ in \cref{lem:annular} is the only place the amplitude enters
the coercivity, and any smaller absolute $\eta_G$ is admissible, which is all
\cref{sec:main} requires since the inset amplitude is $O(\dT^{1/8})\to0$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The translation mode: reduction of the mode-one hypothesis}
\label{ss:translation}

We record what the hypothesis \eqref{eq:mode1hyp} is really controlling, and
how far one can go without it. The asymmetry side requires no mode-one
assumption at all: optimising the comparison disc over translations removes the
first Fourier mode automatically, up to an error of the natural quadratic
order.

\begin{lemma}[Translation-reduced asymmetry]\label{lem:shift-asym}
Let $G=G(0,1,\varphi)$ be normalised as in \eqref{eq:norm}, with $\varphi$
measurable, $\|\varphi\|_{L^\infty}\le\eta_G\le\tfrac1{13}$, and the volume
constraint \eqref{eq:constraints}. Let $q\in\R^2$ be the first Fourier
coefficient vector of $\varphi$, i.e.\ $\omega(\theta)\cdot q=P_1\varphi(\theta)$.
Then $B(q,1)$ is star-shaped about $0$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shift-asym}
   \AF(G)\ \le\ \frac{|G\triangle B(q,1)|}{\pi}
   \ \le\ C\bigl(\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2}+\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2\bigr),
   \qquad\text{hence}\qquad
   \AF(G)^2\ \le\ C\bigl(\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2}^2+\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^4\bigr),
\end{equation}
with $C$ absolute and \emph{no} mode-one hypothesis.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since $|q|=\|P_1\varphi\|_{L^2}/\sqrt\pi\le\|\varphi\|_{L^2}/\sqrt\pi\le
\sqrt2\,\eta_G<1$, the origin lies in $B(q,1)$, which is therefore star-shaped
about $0$ with polar radius $\rho_q(\theta)$ given by $|\rho_q\omega-q|=1$, i.e.
$\rho_q^2-2(\omega\cdot q)\rho_q+|q|^2=1$, so $\rho_q^2=1-|q|^2+2(\omega\cdot q)
\rho_q$ and $\rho_q=(\omega\cdot q)+\sqrt{1-|q|^2+(\omega\cdot q)^2}
=1+\omega\cdot q+O(|q|^2)$. Hence
\[
   \rho_q^2=1-|q|^2+2(\omega\cdot q)+2(\omega\cdot q)^2+r,\qquad
   |r|\le C|q|^3 ,
\]
with $C$ absolute for $|q|\le\sqrt2/13$. Both $G$ and $B(q,1)$ are star-shaped
about $0$ with measurable radial profiles $R=1+\varphi$ and $\rho_q$, so the
polar area formula gives
\[
   |G\triangle B(q,1)|=\tfrac12\int_{S^1}\bigl|R^2-\rho_q^2\bigr|\d\theta,
   \qquad
   R^2-\rho_q^2=2(\varphi-\omega\cdot q)+\varphi^2+|q|^2-2(\omega\cdot q)^2-r .
\]
Therefore, by the triangle inequality and $\omega\cdot q=P_1\varphi$,
\[
   |G\triangle B(q,1)|
   \ \le\ \int_{S^1}|\varphi-P_1\varphi|\d\theta
   +\tfrac12\!\int_{S^1}\!\bigl(\varphi^2+|q|^2+2(\omega\cdot q)^2\bigr)\d\theta
   +\tfrac12\!\int_{S^1}|r|\d\theta .
\]
For the first term, $\varphi-P_1\varphi=P_0\varphi+P_{\ge2}\varphi$, so by
Cauchy--Schwarz $\int_{S^1}|\varphi-P_1\varphi|\le\sqrt{2\pi}\,
(\|P_0\varphi\|_{L^2}+\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2})$; and the volume constraint
gives $\|P_0\varphi\|_{L^2}=\sqrt{2\pi}\,|\overline\varphi|=\tfrac1{2\sqrt{2\pi}}
\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$ (as in \cref{lem:modes}). For the second term,
$\int_{S^1}\varphi^2=\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$, $\int_{S^1}|q|^2=2\pi|q|^2$ and
$\int_{S^1}(\omega\cdot q)^2=\pi|q|^2$, so it equals $\tfrac12\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2
+2\pi|q|^2=\tfrac12\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2+2\|P_1\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$. The third term
is $\le\tfrac12\cdot2\pi C|q|^3\le C\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^3$. Collecting, and using
$\|P_1\varphi\|_{L^2}^2\le\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$ and $\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^3\le
\eta_G\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$,
\[
   |G\triangle B(q,1)|\ \le\ \sqrt{2\pi}\,\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2}
   +C\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2 .
\]
Dividing by $\pi$ (and $\AF(G)\le|G\triangle B(q,1)|/|G|=|G\triangle
B(q,1)|/\pi$) gives the first bound in \eqref{eq:shift-asym}; squaring and using
$2\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|\,\|\varphi\|^2\le\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|^2+\|\varphi\|^4$
gives the second.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[What remains: the translation mode is a quartic direction]
\label{rem:mode1-residual}
\Cref{lem:shift-asym} isolates the exact role of \eqref{eq:mode1hyp}. The
deficit lower bound coerces the modes $k\ge2$: keeping the $k=1$ term in
\cref{lem:annular} (coefficient $c_1(\eta)=\mu/(1+\mu)=1-O(\eta)$) and matching
it against the mode-one part of the moment term \eqref{eq:moment} produces, for
\emph{any} centre and with no mode-one hypothesis, the cancellation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dstar-modes}
   \dstar(G)\ \ge\ c\,\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2}^2-C\,\eta\,\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2 ,
\end{equation}
with $c,C$ absolute. Comparing \eqref{eq:dstar-modes} with the asymmetry bound
\eqref{eq:shift-asym}, the estimate $\AF(G)^2\le C_G\dstar(G)$ would follow from
the single \emph{quartic translation bound}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:quartic-residual}
   \|P_1\varphi\|_{L^2}^4\ \le\ C\,\dstar(G)
   \qquad\bigl(\text{equivalently }\dstar(G)\ge c\,\|P_1\varphi\|_{L^2}^4\bigr),
\end{equation}
which is exactly the statement that translating the disc is a direction of
\emph{vanishing first variation and strictly positive second variation} of the
torsional deficit. The hypothesis \eqref{eq:mode1hyp}, $\|P_1\varphi\|_{L^2}\le
K\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$, implies \eqref{eq:quartic-residual} a fortiori (it makes
$\|\varphi\|_{L^2}^2\le C\|P_{\ge2}\varphi\|_{L^2}^2$, absorbing the
$-C\eta\|\varphi\|^2$ loss in \eqref{eq:dstar-modes}), which is why we adopt it.
We emphasise that \eqref{eq:quartic-residual} is \emph{not} delivered by the
slope-free energy method of this section: the translation contributes to
$\dstar$ only at order $\|P_1\varphi\|^4=O(\eta^2)\|P_1\varphi\|^2$, which lies
below the $O(\eta)\|P_1\varphi\|^2$ loss of the one-sided annular Steklov bound
\cref{lem:annular} and of the cubic remainder in \eqref{eq:moment}; extracting
it would require an exact (rather than one-sided) second-order computation of
the harmonic energy under the domain perturbation. This is the precise content
of the residual recorded as \eqref{eq:mode1-scoped} in \cref{sec:main}.
\end{remark}
