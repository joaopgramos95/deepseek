# High-End Referee Report and Journal Matching

## Summary of Findings

The manuscript is not a routine time-frequency paper. If the main arguments are correct, it combines several high-level ingredients: Bargmann-Fock representation of Gaussian localization operators, inverse spectral construction of domains from prescribed eigenfunctions, weighted quadrature/free-boundary methods, rigidity of Hermite eigenfunctions, sharpness of quantitative Faber-Krahn stability, local maximizer rigidity, and a concentration-compactness route to Nicola-Tilli. This is substantially above the average technical and conceptual level of specialist journals such as ACHA or JFAA; those should be viewed as safe/fallback venues, not as the natural ceiling.

## Referee-Level Evaluation

- **Originality:** Very high. The perturbative inverse theorem near the Gaussian is the central novelty. Constructing localization domains from prescribed near-Gaussian eigenfunctions is a genuinely structural result, not an incremental estimate.

- **Technical depth:** High to very high. The paper mixes time-frequency analysis, Fock-space operator theory, inverse potential theory, quadrature domains, free boundaries, spectral perturbation, and variational concentration. This range of techniques is closer to the profile of strong general analysis journals than to ordinary applied harmonic-analysis outlets.

- **Conceptual contribution:** The strongest conceptual point is the slogan that eigenfunctions are geometric data for Gaussian localization operators. The paper does not merely prove another STFT inequality; it proposes an inverse/free-boundary mechanism behind the geometry of localization domains.

- **Strategic weakness:** The paper is very broad. For a top journal this is acceptable only if the exposition makes the central narrative unmistakable: inverse spectral construction first, then rigidity and variational consequences as evidence of the framework's power.

- **Main referee risk:** Not the level of the result, but credibility of proof execution. High-end journals will tolerate length and technicality if the architecture is clean; they will not tolerate black-box transitions where the novelty is supposed to occur.

## Specialist Journals As Fallbacks, Not Primary Targets

- **Applied and Computational Harmonic Analysis:** Excellent topical fit, but likely below the natural ambition if the full theorem package is correct.

- **Journal of Functional Analysis:** A respectable fallback, especially if the paper is reframed around Fock-space operators and spectral theory. Not the ceiling.

- **Journal of Fourier Analysis and Applications:** Too specialized and probably too low for the full manuscript if the main inverse theorem and applications are all correct.

- **Inverse Problems:** Topically relevant to the inverse construction, but the full paper is broader and more structural than the average journal fit there.

## Recommended Submission Strategy

1. **Aim high first.** A reasonable sequence is `Inventiones` or `Duke` first, then `JEMS` / `Analysis & PDE` / `CPAM` depending on editorial feedback and how the paper is framed.

2. **Do not lead with ACHA/JFAA.** Those journals are good homes for ordinary strong time-frequency papers, but this manuscript, if correct, is selling a new inverse/free-boundary framework.

3. **Make the narrative less local.** The introduction should not read as "we prove several things about localization operators". It should read as "we introduce an inverse free-boundary method for spectral geometry in the Bargmann-Fock model, resolving rigidity, construction, stability sharpness, and extremizer questions."

4. **Package the theorem hierarchy carefully.** The perturbative inverse construction should be the flagship theorem. Abreu-Doerfler recovery, GGRT sharpness, local maximizers, and Nicola-Tilli recovery should be presented as consequences of the new framework.

5. **Polish the proof architecture before top submission.** At this level, the paper can be long, but every external input must be precisely stated and every reduction should be encapsulated in a named proposition or lemma.

## Suggested Positioning

The strongest pitch is:

> This paper develops an inverse free-boundary theory for Gaussian time-frequency localization operators. It shows that near-Gaussian eigenfunctions can be prescribed by constructing localization domains, recovers classical rigidity, proves sharpness of quantitative stability, and gives a variational route to disk extremizers in the Bargmann-Fock model.

This pitch is not ACHA-level in ambition. It is a general-analysis pitch.

## Bottom Line

If the main theorem package is correct, the paper should first be aimed at a high-end general analysis journal. **ACHA/JFAA/JFA are fallback venues, not the intended level.** The decisive issue is not whether the content is strong enough in principle; it is whether the manuscript is polished enough that a top referee can verify the free-boundary and inverse-spectral mechanisms without reconstructing the paper independently.
