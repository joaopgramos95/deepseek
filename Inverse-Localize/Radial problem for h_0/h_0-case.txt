Yes. In the (h_0)-window, (h_0)-eigenfunction, simply connected case, there is a very plausible route, and in fact it gives something stronger than the Garofalo–Segala hypothesis.

The key point is that in this case the inverse-function issue becomes much simpler than for general (h_k), because the primitive (\psi_0) has no critical points.

Let me write it carefully.

---

## 1. The PDE in the (h_0), (h_0) case

The Bargmann identity is
[
\int_U e^{\pi z\bar w}e^{-\pi |w|^2},dA(w)=\lambda
\qquad (\forall z\in \C).
]

The associated compactly supported potential satisfies
[
\Delta u = e^{-\pi |z|^2}\mathbf 1_U - c,\delta_0
]
for some constant (c), and (u\equiv 0) in the unbounded component of (\C\setminus \overline U).

Since (U) is bounded and simply connected, (\C\setminus \overline U) has only the unbounded component. So outside (U),
[
u\equiv 0.
]

Also, (0\in U). Indeed, if (0\notin U), then (0) lies in the exterior, where (u\equiv 0) in a neighborhood of (0), which is incompatible with the (-c\delta_0) term unless (c=0), which is not the relevant case.

So in the simply connected case, the singularity is inside (U), and the whole boundary is the outer boundary.

---

## 2. Outer-boundary regularity

Near any boundary point, since the exterior side is identically zero, the problem is locally one-phase:
[
\Delta u=e^{-\pi |z|^2}\quad \text{in }U \text{ near }\partial U,
\qquad
u=0,\ \nabla u=0\quad \text{on }\partial U.
]

Because the forcing (e^{-\pi |z|^2}) is real-analytic and strictly positive, standard one-phase free-boundary regularity gives that (\partial U) is real-analytic.

So the classical Schwarz function (S) of (\partial U) exists in a collar neighborhood of the boundary.

---

## 3. The right holomorphic quantity

Define
[
\Psi_0(z,\bar z):=\frac{1-e^{-\pi z\bar z}}{\pi z}.
]

A direct calculation gives
[
\partial_{\bar z}\Psi_0(z,\bar z)=e^{-\pi |z|^2}.
]

Now set
[
F(z):=4\partial_z u(z)-\Psi_0(z,\bar z)+\frac{c}{\pi z}.
]

Then, in (U\setminus{0}),
[
\partial_{\bar z}F
==================

\Delta u - e^{-\pi |z|^2}+c,\delta_0
=0.
]

Near (0), the singular part of (u) is
[
u(z)=\frac{c}{2\pi}\log|z| + h(z),
]
with (h) harmonic, so
[
4\partial_z u(z)=\frac{c}{\pi z}+4\partial_z h(z).
]

Since (\Psi_0) is smooth at (0) and the singular term is canceled by (c/(\pi z)), the singularity is removable. Hence:

[
\boxed{F \text{ is holomorphic on all of }U.}
]

---

## 4. Boundary values of (F)

On (\partial U), because (u\equiv 0) on the exterior side and (u\in C^1) across the analytic free boundary, one has
[
\nabla u=0 \quad \text{on }\partial U,
]
hence
[
\partial_z u=0 \quad \text{on }\partial U.
]

Therefore on (\partial U),
[
F(z)= -\Psi_0(z,\bar z)+\frac{c}{\pi z}
=======================================

-\frac{1-e^{-\pi |z|^2}}{\pi z}+\frac{c}{\pi z}.
]

Multiply by (\pi z) and define
[
W(z):=1-c+\pi zF(z).
]

Then (W) is holomorphic in (U), and on the boundary,
[
W(z)=e^{-\pi |z|^2}.
]

So:

[
\boxed{
W \text{ is holomorphic in }U,\qquad
W|_{\partial U}=e^{-\pi |z|^2}\in (0,1)\subset \R.
}
]

---

## 5. The decisive step

Because (W) is holomorphic in (U) and continuous up to the real-analytic boundary, its imaginary part is harmonic in (U) and vanishes on (\partial U). Therefore
[
\Im W \equiv 0 \quad \text{in }U.
]

So (W) is real-valued and holomorphic in (U), hence constant:
[
W(z)\equiv \kappa
]
for some (\kappa\in (0,1)).

Restricting to the boundary,
[
e^{-\pi |z|^2}=\kappa
\qquad \text{on }\partial U.
]

Therefore
[
|z|^2 = -\frac{1}{\pi}\log \kappa =: R^2
\qquad \text{on }\partial U.
]

So (\partial U) is the circle (|z|=R). Since (U) is bounded and simply connected,

[
\boxed{U={,|z|<R,}.}
]

So in this case you do not merely get the G–S continuation hypothesis; you get the full conclusion directly.

---

## 6. Relation to the Schwarz function

Since (\partial U) is analytic, let (S) be its classical Schwarz function near the boundary. On (\partial U),
[
zS(z)=|z|^2=R^2.
]

Hence near the boundary,
[
S(z)=\frac{R^2}{z}.
]

So the Schwarz function extends meromorphically into all of (U), with a single simple pole at (0). This is much stronger than the Garofalo–Segala assumption, which only asks for a one-sided holomorphic/meromorphic continuation into a region with a pole. 

---

## 7. What this means for your question

So the answer is:

[
\boxed{
\text{Yes — in the }h_0\text{ window, }h_0\text{ eigenfunction, simply connected case, the PDE/implicit-Schwarz structure should imply the G–S condition automatically, and in fact it should force }U\text{ to be a disk directly.}
}
]

The only real analytic inputs are:

* the compactly supported potential formulation,
* the one-phase boundary regularity for the outer boundary,
* the fact that (u=0) outside (U), hence (\nabla u=0) on (\partial U),
* the holomorphicity of (F).

Once those are in place, the argument above is very short.

## 8. The real caveat

The caveat is not conceptual but technical: one must make sure the passage from the localization identity to the classical PDE/free-boundary formulation is rigorous enough, and that (F) really extends holomorphically across (0) exactly as stated.

But assuming the standard PDE setup you were already using for the Gaussian-window case, the simply connected (k=0) case looks substantially easier than the general (k\ge 1) case.

So this is not just “there is a chance.” It looks like the right argument.
